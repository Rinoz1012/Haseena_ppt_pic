Main Notes
Early Regulatory Acts
The Regulating Act of 1773 represented the first British parliamentary attempt to regulate and oversee the East India Company's affairs in India, responding to concerns about the Company's unchecked power and financial mismanagement!
Pitt's India Act (1784)
This Act established a system of dual control — the Company retained its commercial trading functions, while a Crown-appointed Board of Control was given oversight of political and administrative matters, marking increased government involvement in Indian affairs!
The Charter Acts
A series of Charter Acts (1793, 1813, 1833, and 1853) periodically renewed and modified the Company's operating charter, each time progressively reducing its trade monopoly and commercial privileges!
The Charter Act of 1833 proved particularly significant, ending the Company's remaining trade monopoly entirely and transforming it into a purely administrative body governing India on behalf of the British Crown, rather than a commercial trading enterprise!
Land Revenue Systems
The British administration implemented different land revenue collection systems across various regions — the Permanent Settlement in Bengal (1793, fixing revenue demands permanently with zamindars), the Ryotwari System in Madras and Bombay presidencies (direct revenue collection from individual cultivators), and the Mahalwari System in North India (revenue settlements with village communities)!
Economic Policies
Overall, British economic policies during this period generally served British commercial and industrial interests, often coming at significant expense to Indian producers, traditional industries, and indigenous economic structures!
Summary
Between 1757-1857, the British government progressively increased its regulatory control over the East India Company through the Regulating Act (1773), Pitt's India Act (1784, establishing dual control), and a series of Charter Acts (1793-1853) that steadily eroded the Company's trade monopoly — culminating in the Charter Act of 1833, which ended commercial functions entirely and made the Company purely administrative. During this period, the British also implemented distinct land revenue systems across India (Permanent Settlement in Bengal, Ryotwari in Madras/Bombay, Mahalwari in North India), while broader economic policies generally prioritized British commercial and industrial interests, often at significant cost to indigenous Indian producers and industries.
