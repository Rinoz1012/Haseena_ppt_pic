Main Notes
Administrative Structure
The British established a centralized administrative bureaucracy in India, with the Governor-General at its apex, and provincial administration conducted under Governors in the various presidencies and provinces!
Civil Services and Judicial System
Over this period, the British developed a professional civil service to administer India, initially staffed exclusively by British officials, based on principles of merit and structured training, though excluding Indians from higher positions for a considerable time!
A hierarchy of courts was established, blending British legal principles and procedures with some degree of accommodation for existing Indian personal laws in matters of religion, marriage, and inheritance!
Social Policy
British social policy during this period reflected a somewhat mixed approach — some significant reforms were undertaken, such as the abolition of practices like sati, while a general policy of non-interference in religious and social matters was maintained in many other areas, largely to avoid provoking unrest among the Indian population!
Cultural and Educational Policy
This period saw the introduction of English-medium education, significantly influenced by Thomas Macaulay's advocacy; a notable debate unfolded between "Orientalists" (who favored promoting traditional Indian learning) and "Anglicists" (who favored English education and Western knowledge), with the Anglicist position eventually prevailing in official policy!
Missionary Activity
Christian missionary activity was permitted and at times indirectly supported by British authorities, contributing to certain social changes in India while also occasionally generating tension and resentment among the broader population!
Summary
British administrative organisation in India featured a centralized bureaucracy headed by the Governor-General, with provincial governance under Governors, supported by a developing (initially exclusively British) professional civil service and a hybrid judicial system blending British law with Indian personal law accommodations. Social policy reflected tension between selective reform (such as abolishing sati) and general non-interference to avoid unrest, while cultural policy saw the introduction of English education following the Orientalist-Anglicist debate, ultimately resolved in favor of English-medium instruction under Macaulay's influence. Christian missionary activity, permitted throughout this period, contributed to social change while also generating periodic tensions.
