Main Notes
Meaning and Types
A political party is an organized group of citizens who hold common political views and seek to capture political power through constitutional means, primarily elections!
Party systems are classified as one-party (single dominant/only party), two-party (two major parties alternating in power, e.g. USA, UK), and multi-party (several parties, often requiring coalitions to form government)!
Party System in India
India follows a multi-party system, reflecting its diversity of region, religion, language, caste, and ideology!
Evolved from a Congress-dominant system in the 1950s-60s to a more fragmented, coalition-driven multi-party system especially from 1989 onward, with regional parties playing an increasingly significant role!
Recognition of National and State Parties
The Election Commission of India, under the Election Symbols (Reservation and Allotment) Order, 1968, grants recognition as a National Party if a party satisfies any one of these: securing at least 6% of valid votes in 4 or more states in a general election AND winning at least 4 Lok Sabha seats; OR winning at least 2% of total Lok Sabha seats from at least 3 different states; OR being recognized as a State Party in at least 4 states!
Recognition as a State Party requires satisfying any one of: securing at least 6% of valid votes in the state along with winning 2 seats in the Lok Sabha or State Assembly from that state; OR winning at least 3% of the total seats in the State Assembly (subject to a minimum of 3 seats); OR winning 1 Lok Sabha seat for every 25 seats (or any fraction thereof) allotted to that state; OR securing at least 8% of the total valid votes in the state in any election (Lok Sabha or Assembly)!
Recognized parties benefit from a reserved election symbol across the country/state, free broadcast time on state-run television and radio during elections, and access to electoral rolls — benefits not available to parties that are merely registered but not recognized!
Summary
Political parties are classified into one-party, two-party, and multi-party systems; India follows a multi-party system that evolved from Congress dominance to a coalition-driven landscape post-1989. The Election Commission grants National Party status based on vote-share/seat thresholds across multiple states (e.g., 6% votes in 4+ states with 4 LS seats), and State Party status based on similar but state-specific thresholds (e.g., 6% votes plus 2 seats, or 8% votes in any election). Recognition brings tangible benefits — reserved symbols, free broadcast time, and electoral roll access — distinguishing recognized parties from the many merely registered ones.
