Main Notes
The Turning Point and Weak Successors
The death of Aurangzeb in 1707 is conventionally taken as the starting point of the Mughal Empire's decline — by this time, the empire had grown vast and increasingly overstretched, straining its administrative and military capacity!
A rapid succession of weak and ineffective rulers followed Aurangzeb, with the imperial court increasingly dominated by factional rivalries among competing nobles!
Structural Economic Causes
The Jagirdari crisis — a growing shortage of available jagirs (land revenue assignments) relative to the number of nobles requiring them — fueled intense court rivalry and corruption, undermining effective governance!
Historian Irfan Habib has argued that an underlying agrarian crisis, driven by over-taxation of the peasantry, led to widespread agrarian unrest and revolts, which progressively undermined the empire's revenue base and administrative stability!
Foreign Invasions
Nadir Shah's invasion from Persia in 1739 dealt a severe blow, sacking Delhi and carrying away immense wealth, including the famous Peacock Throne and the Koh-i-Noor diamond!
Ahmad Shah Abdali, an Afghan invader, launched multiple subsequent raids into India, further weakening the already fragile Mughal state!
Rise of Regional Successor States
As central Mughal authority weakened, several regional successor states emerged with increasing autonomy — including Bengal, Awadh, and Hyderabad — which, while nominally still under Mughal suzerainty, functioned as effectively independent regional powers!
The Marathas expanded aggressively into former Mughal territories during this period, further accelerating the decline of central Mughal authority across large parts of India!
Historiographical Debate
Historians continue to debate the primary cause of Mughal decline — some, like Irfan Habib, emphasize the underlying agrarian/economic crisis as the fundamental driver, while others place greater weight on political and administrative failures at court, and still others emphasize the destabilizing impact of repeated foreign invasions!
Most modern historians view the decline as genuinely multi-causal, resulting from the interaction of these economic, political, and military factors rather than any single cause!
Summary
The decline of the Mughal Empire, conventionally dated from Aurangzeb's death (1707), resulted from a combination of factors: weak successive rulers and factional court politics, the structural Jagirdari crisis (shortage of land assignments for nobles), an underlying agrarian crisis (per Irfan Habib's thesis of over-taxation-driven peasant unrest), devastating foreign invasions (Nadir Shah's 1739 sack of Delhi taking the Peacock Throne and Koh-i-Noor, and Ahmad Shah Abdali's subsequent raids), and the rise of increasingly autonomous regional successor states (Bengal, Awadh, Hyderabad) alongside aggressive Maratha expansion. Historians continue to debate the relative weight of economic, political, and military causes, generally converging on a multi-causal explanation for this pivotal transformation in Indian history.
