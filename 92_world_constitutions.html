Main Notes
American Constitution
The US Constitution is a written, codified document — the shortest national constitution in the world — and is rigid, requiring a special amendment procedure!
It establishes a federal structure with a presidential system of government, based on a strict separation of powers among the executive, legislature, and judiciary!
The legislature (Congress) is bicameral, comprising the Senate and the House of Representatives; American citizens hold dual citizenship (federal and state), unlike India's single citizenship!
The first 10 amendments constitute the Bill of Rights, protecting fundamental civil liberties!
American President
The US President combines the roles of head of state and head of government, unlike India's split between the President (head of state) and PM (head of government)!
Elected indirectly through the Electoral College system, for a 4-year term, with a maximum of two terms permitted under the 22nd Amendment!
The President is not responsible to Congress and cannot be removed through a no-confidence vote — the Cabinet serves purely in an advisory capacity, with no principle of collective responsibility as seen in parliamentary systems!
British Constitution
The British Constitution is unwritten (uncodified) and flexible, having evolved gradually over centuries rather than being drafted at a single point in time!
It establishes a unitary state, and is founded on the principle of Parliamentary sovereignty — Parliament's laws cannot be struck down by courts through judicial review, unlike in India!
Constitutional conventions play a significant role in governing the practical functioning of the British system, and it follows a fusion (rather than separation) of powers, with the executive drawn from and accountable to the legislature!
British Cabinet
The British Cabinet is the real executive authority, headed by the Prime Minister, who functions as "primus inter pares" (first among equals) rather than a dominant, unchecked leader!
It operates on the principle of collective responsibility — the Cabinet as a whole is accountable to Parliament for its decisions — and is drawn from the majority party in the House of Commons, with secrecy maintained over Cabinet proceedings!
French Constitution
France follows a semi-presidential (dual executive) system under the Fifth Republic Constitution (1958), where both the President and the Prime Minister hold real executive power!
This system allows for "cohabitation," where the President and Prime Minister may belong to different, even opposing, political parties simultaneously!
French President
The French President is directly elected by the people (since 1962), and serves a term of 5 years (reduced from the earlier 7-year term since 2000)!
The President holds strong powers, including the ability to dissolve the National Assembly and invoke emergency powers under Article 16, while the Prime Minister generally handles domestic governance and remains accountable to the National Assembly!
Japanese Constitution
Japan's current Constitution, adopted in 1947 in the aftermath of World War II, is often called the "Postwar Constitution"!
The Emperor serves as a purely symbolic head of state without real governing power, while the Prime Minister, operating within a parliamentary system, holds actual executive authority!
Article 9, a distinctive pacifist clause, renounces war as a sovereign right of the nation and prohibits the maintenance of armed forces for war-making purposes!
The legislature (the Diet) is bicameral, comprising the House of Representatives and the House of Councillors!
Soviet Constitution
The Soviet Union was governed successively by the 1936 Constitution (under Stalin) and the 1977 Constitution (under Brezhnev), both establishing a single-party state under the Communist Party!
The Supreme Soviet functioned as the nominal legislature, but real power rested with the Communist Party leadership; the state was federal in form but functioned as a highly centralized, unitary system in practice, dominated entirely by the CPSU — the USSR dissolved in 1991!
Russian Constitution
The current Russian Constitution, adopted in 1993 in the post-Soviet era, establishes a semi-presidential system!
The President is directly elected and holds very substantial power, with a 6-year term following a 2008 constitutional amendment; the Prime Minister heads the government but remains subordinate to the President!
The legislature, the Federal Assembly, is bicameral, comprising the State Duma and the Federation Council!
Chinese Constitution
China's current Constitution, adopted in 1982 and amended several times since, establishes a single-party state led by the Communist Party of China (CPC)!
The National People's Congress (NPC) is nominally the supreme organ of state power, but real authority rests with the CPC leadership, especially the President (who typically also serves as CPC General Secretary); China remains a unitary state!
Swiss Constitution
Switzerland has a federal constitutional structure with strong decentralization, and is notable for its extensive use of direct democracy mechanisms — referendums and citizen initiatives — to shape law and policy!
Its executive branch is a unique 7-member collegial body called the Federal Council, which exercises collective headship of state, with the ceremonial position of President rotating annually among its members!
Switzerland is also known for its long-standing tradition of political and military neutrality in foreign affairs!
Summary
This chapter compares constitutional systems worldwide: the US Constitution (written, rigid, federal, presidential, dual citizenship) and its President (combined head of state/govt, Electoral College-elected, fixed term); the UK's unwritten, flexible, unitary system built on Parliamentary sovereignty and a collectively responsible Cabinet led by a "first among equals" PM; France's semi-presidential dual executive (President with strong emergency/dissolution powers, PM handling domestic affairs, cohabitation possible); Japan's postwar Constitution with a symbolic Emperor, real-power PM, and the pacifist Article 9; the Soviet Union's single-party, CPSU-dominated system (1936/1977 Constitutions, dissolved 1991) succeeded by Russia's 1993 semi-presidential Constitution with a powerful directly-elected President; China's 1982 Constitution and CPC-led unitary single-party system; and Switzerland's uniquely collegial 7-member Federal Council executive combined with extensive direct democracy and a tradition of neutrality.
