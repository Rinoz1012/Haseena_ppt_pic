Main Notes
Background
The National Commission to Review the Working of the Constitution (NCRWC) was constituted in 2000 by the NDA government (under PM Atal Bihari Vajpayee)!
It was chaired by Justice M.N. Venkatachaliah, a former Chief Justice of India, and submitted its report in 2002!
Terms of Reference
The Commission was tasked with examining how the Constitution had worked over the preceding 50 years, and recommending changes needed for more effective governance!
Importantly, its mandate was explicitly limited to functional and operational improvements, consistent with the basic structure of the Constitution — it was NOT meant to reopen or alter the Constitution's foundational framework!
Areas of Concern
The Commission examined issues relating to the stability of government, particularly given the frequent instability associated with coalition politics!
It also looked at Centre-State (federal) relations and tensions, the need for electoral system reforms, and the growing politicization of the civil services and bureaucracy!
Other areas of concern included persistent delays in justice delivery, the criminalization of politics, tensions in the executive-legislature relationship, and the effective implementation of Fundamental Rights and Directive Principles!
Recommendations
The Commission recommended consideration of a fixed tenure for the Lok Sabha and State Legislative Assemblies to reduce political instability caused by frequent, premature dissolutions!
It recommended reforms to strengthen the Anti-Defection Law and address its shortcomings!
It also called for strengthening local self-government institutions (panchayats and municipalities), pursuing judicial reforms to reduce case pendency, and considering electoral reforms including the question of state funding of elections!
Summary
The NCRWC was set up in 2000 by the Vajpayee government, chaired by former CJI Justice M.N. Venkatachaliah, and submitted its report in 2002. Its terms of reference restricted it to examining the Constitution's functional working over 50 years and recommending operational improvements consistent with the basic structure — not altering the foundational framework. Areas of concern included government stability, Centre-State relations, electoral reforms, bureaucratic politicization, judicial delays, criminalization of politics, and effective rights implementation. Key recommendations included considering fixed tenures for legislatures, strengthening the Anti-Defection Law, bolstering local self-government, pursuing judicial reforms, and exploring state funding of elections.
