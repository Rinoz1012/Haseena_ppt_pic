Main Notes
The Struggle for Domination in North India
The 8th-10th centuries saw a prolonged three-way contest — the "Tripartite Struggle" — between the Palas, Pratiharas, and Rashtrakutas for control over Kanauj in the Gangetic plains, which was seen as symbolically important for claiming paramountcy over North India!
The Pratiharas
Also known as the Gurjara-Pratiharas, they were based in Rajasthan and later controlled parts of the Gangetic plains!
Ruler Nagabhata is notable for resisting early Arab incursions into western India, helping check the spread of Arab power beyond Sindh for a period!
The Rashtrakutas
Centered in the Deccan with their capital at Manyakheta, the Rashtrakutas were significant patrons of art and religion, supporting both Jainism and Shaivism!
They are especially remembered for commissioning the remarkable Kailasa temple at Ellora, carved entirely out of a single rock!
Political Ideas and Organisation
Kingship during this period remained largely regionally rooted rather than centralizing into a single unified empire!
Despite the intensity of the Tripartite Struggle, no single power succeeded in establishing lasting pan-Indian paramountcy, and all three empires eventually weakened, paving the way for later regional successor states!
Summary
The period 800-1000 CE in North India was dominated by the prolonged Tripartite Struggle among the Palas (Bengal/Bihar, Buddhist patrons), the Pratiharas (Rajasthan/Gangetic plains, resisted early Arab incursions), and the Rashtrakutas (Deccan, patrons of Jainism/Shaivism, built the Kailasa temple at Ellora) — all contesting control of the symbolically important city of Kanauj. Despite this intense rivalry, none achieved lasting pan-Indian dominance, and political power remained fundamentally regional in character.
