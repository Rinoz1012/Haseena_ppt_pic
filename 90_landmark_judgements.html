90. Landmark Judgements & Their Impact — Cornell Notes 06 Jul 2026
Grouped by theme for faster recall — most-tested cases are marked with a ★.
A — Early Fundamental Rights Cases (1950-51)
Romesh Thappar (1950) — Freedom of press is part of Art 19(1)(a) free speech; state can't ban a publication merely to secure "public order" without threat to security.
A.K. Gopalan (1950) — Early narrow view: Fundamental Rights are watertight compartments; "procedure established by law" (Art 21) just needs valid enacted procedure, no fairness test (later overruled in spirit by Maneka Gandhi).
Champakam Dorairajan (1951) — Caste-based reservation in education struck down as violating Art 29(2); led directly to the First Amendment adding Art 15(4).
★ Shankari Prasad (1951) — Parliament CAN amend Fundamental Rights under Art 368; "law" in Art 13(2) doesn't include constitutional amendments.
B — Basic Structure Doctrine Evolution
Berubari Union (1960) — Advisory opinion: ceding Indian territory to another country requires a constitutional amendment, not just an ordinary law.
I.C. Golak Nath (1967) — Reversed Shankari Prasad: Parliament CANNOT amend Fundamental Rights via Art 368; applied prospective overruling for the first time.
★ Kesavananda Bharati (1973) — Landmark: Parliament CAN amend any part incl. FRs, but CANNOT alter the "Basic Structure" of the Constitution. 13-judge bench, largest ever.
★ Minerva Mills (1980) — Struck down 42nd Amendment's attempt to give unlimited amending power to Parliament; judicial review = part of basic structure.
Waman Rao (1980) — Basic structure doctrine applies to amendments AFTER 24 April 1973 (Kesavananda date), not retrospectively before it.
I.R. Coelho (2007) — Laws placed in 9th Schedule after 24 April 1973 are subject to judicial review if they violate basic structure/FRs.
C — Emergency & Political Crisis Cases
Indira Nehru Gandhi v Raj Narain (1975) — Struck down a constitutional amendment clause protecting PM's election from judicial scrutiny — violated free/fair elections (basic structure).
★ A.D.M. Jabalpur (1976) — "Habeas Corpus case": held citizens have NO right to approach courts for Art 21 enforcement during Emergency — widely criticized, effectively overruled later (esp. in Puttaswamy).
S.R. Bommai (1994) — Laid down guidelines curbing arbitrary use of Art 356 (President's Rule); floor test in Assembly = proper test of majority, not Governor's subjective satisfaction.
D — Expansion of Article 21 (Right to Life)
★ Maneka Gandhi (1978) — Overturned Gopalan's narrow view: "procedure established by law" under Art 21 must be fair, just, and reasonable, not arbitrary — linked Art 14, 19, 21 together.
Bachan Singh (1980) — Upheld death penalty's constitutionality but restricted it to "rarest of rare" cases.
Mohini Jain (1992) & Unni Krishnan (1993) — Right to education held to flow from Art 21 (later became a distinct Fundamental Right via 86th Amendment/Art 21A).
Vishaka (1997) — Laid down guidelines against sexual harassment at workplace, in absence of specific law (later became basis for the 2013 POSH Act).
Aruna Ramachandra Shanbaug (2011) — Recognized passive euthanasia in limited circumstances, subject to safeguards (paved way for later "living will" recognition).
★ K.S. Puttaswamy (2017) — Right to Privacy declared a Fundamental Right under Art 21; foundational for later Aadhaar/data-protection jurisprudence.
E — Federalism, Institutions & Governance
D.C. Wadhwa (1986) — Re-promulgation of ordinances without placing them before legislature = fraud on the Constitution, unconstitutional.
Kihoto Hollohan (1992) — Upheld 10th Schedule (Anti-Defection Law); Speaker's decision on disqualification is subject to judicial review.
SC Advocates-on-Record Assn (1993 & 2015) — 1993: established the Judges' Collegium system for SC/HC appointments; 2015: struck down NJAC (99th Amendment) as unconstitutional, restoring Collegium.
Vineet Narain (1997) — Directed CBI/CVC insulation from executive interference; led to CVC being given statutory status (2003).
Prakash Singh (2006) — Directed police reforms — fixed tenure for DGPs/SPs, separation of investigation from law-and-order functions, State Security Commissions.
T.S.R. Subramanian (2013) — Directed fixed minimum tenure for civil servants and a Civil Services Board to insulate bureaucracy from political interference.
F — Reservation & Equality
★ Indra Sawhney (1992) — "Mandal case": upheld 27% OBC reservation; capped total reservation at 50% (with exceptions); excluded "creamy layer" from OBC reservation; no reservation in promotions (initially).
M. Nagaraj (2006) — Upheld constitutional validity of reservation in promotions for SC/ST (via 77th/85th Amendments), subject to conditions (backwardness, inadequate representation, administrative efficiency).
G — Personal Liberty, Dignity & Social Justice (Recent)
Shah Bano (1985) — Muslim woman entitled to maintenance under Sec 125 CrPC beyond personal law; triggered political controversy and the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986.
Lily Thomas (2013) — Struck down the provision allowing convicted MPs/MLAs to continue in office pending appeal; immediate disqualification on conviction.
National Legal Services Authority (2014) — Recognized transgender persons as a "third gender," with rights under Art 14, 15, 16, 19, 21.
Shayara Bano (2017) — Struck down instant "Triple Talaq" (talaq-e-biddat) as unconstitutional; led to the Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Marriage) Act, 2019.
Joseph Shine (2018) — Struck down Sec 497 IPC (adultery) as unconstitutional — discriminatory and treated women as property.
★ Navtej Singh Johar (2018) — Decriminalized consensual homosexual acts by reading down Sec 377 IPC; upheld right to sexual orientation/privacy/dignity.
Indian Young Lawyers Association (2018) — "Sabarimala case": held banning women (10-50 yrs) from Sabarimala temple entry unconstitutional (later referred to a larger bench for review).
M. Siddiq (2019) — Ayodhya/Ram Janmabhoomi title dispute resolved in favour of the deity's title; separate land allotted for a mosque.
H — Electoral Transparency & Digital Rights
★ Association for Democratic Reforms (2002) — Voters have a right to know candidates' criminal, financial, and educational background before voting (Art 19(1)(a)).
Naveen Jindal (2004) — Right to fly the national flag with dignity/respect is a fundamental right under Art 19(1)(a), subject to Flag Code regulation.
People's Union for Civil Liberties (2013) — Introduced NOTA (None of the Above) option on EVMs, protecting voters' right to secrecy of choice not to vote for anyone.
Shreya Singhal (2015) — Struck down Section 66A of the IT Act as unconstitutional — vague, overbroad restriction on online free speech.
Rambabu Singh Thakur (2020) — Directed political parties to publish criminal antecedents of candidates within 48 hours of selection, and the reasons for selecting them despite pending cases.
Internet and Mobile Association of India (2020) — Struck down RBI's banking ban on cryptocurrency transactions for being disproportionate.
I — Miscellaneous Notable Cases
K.M. Nanavati (1961) — Famous criminal case on the defence of "grave and sudden provocation" in murder; also notable for role of jury trial (later abolished in India).
T.M.A. Pai Foundation (2002) — Laid down principles on autonomy of private educational institutions (including minority institutions) in admissions and fee structure.
M.C. Mehta (1986) — Landmark environmental litigation (Oleum Gas Leak case); evolved the "Absolute Liability" principle for hazardous industries, stricter than "strict liability."
Anuradha Bhasin (2020) — Held that indefinite suspension of internet services (in J&K) violates free speech/trade; internet access linked to Art 19 freedoms, restrictions must pass proportionality test.
High-value memory anchors: Kesavananda(1973)=Basic Structure | Maneka Gandhi(1978)=Due Process reading into Art 21 | Indra Sawhney(1992)=Mandal/50% cap | Puttaswamy(2017)=Privacy as FR | Navtej Johar(2018)=Sec 377 struck down
Summary
These landmark judgements trace India's constitutional evolution across key themes: early narrow FR interpretation (Gopalan) giving way to expansive Art 21 jurisprudence (Maneka Gandhi, Puttaswamy); the Basic Structure doctrine emerging through Golak Nath → Kesavananda Bharati → Minerva Mills → Waman Rao → I.R. Coelho; Emergency-era setbacks (ADM Jabalpur) followed by course-correction (Bommai's curbs on Art 356); institutional reforms (Vineet Narain on CBI/CVC, Prakash Singh on police, Collegium cases on judicial appointments); reservation jurisprudence (Indra Sawhney's 50% cap, Nagaraj on promotions); and a wave of dignity/personal liberty judgements since 2013 (Lily Thomas, NALSA, Shayara Bano, Joseph Shine, Navtej Johar, Sabarimala, Ayodhya) alongside electoral transparency (ADR, PUCL/NOTA, Rambabu Singh Thakur) and digital-age rights (Shreya Singhal, Anuradha Bhasin, IAMAI on crypto).
