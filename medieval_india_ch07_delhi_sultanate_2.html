Main Notes
The Khaljis (1290-1320)
The Khalji dynasty succeeded the Mamluks, with Jalaluddin Khalji founding the dynasty. Alauddin Khalji stands out as its most significant ruler, implementing strict market control and price regulation reforms to support a large standing army, successfully defending against repeated Mongol invasions, and extending Sultanate influence into the Deccan through campaigns led by his general Malik Kafur!
The Tughlaqs (1320-1412)
The Tughlaq dynasty succeeded the Khaljis, founded by Ghiyasuddin Tughlaq. His successor, Muhammad bin Tughlaq, is remembered as the most controversial ruler of the Sultanate due to several bold but ultimately failed administrative experiments!
Muhammad Tughlaq's Experiments
He attempted to shift the capital from Delhi to Daulatabad in the Deccan, a move later reversed due to practical difficulties!
He introduced a token currency system using copper and brass coins valued equivalent to silver, which failed due to widespread forgery and lack of public trust!
He also significantly increased taxation in the fertile Doab region, which led to famine conditions and provoked rebellion among the peasantry!
Expansion of the Delhi Sultanate
The Sultanate reached its greatest territorial extent during the Khalji-Tughlaq period, with Muhammad bin Tughlaq briefly extending control over a very substantial portion of the Indian subcontinent, before internal problems began undermining this expansion!
Decline and Disintegration
Firuz Shah Tughlaq attempted to stabilize the empire following Muhammad Tughlaq's troubled reign, but the Sultanate began fragmenting significantly under his weaker successors, shrinking considerably in territorial extent by the close of the 14th century!
Summary
The 14th century saw the Delhi Sultanate reach its territorial peak under the Khaljis (notably Alauddin Khalji's market reforms, Mongol defense, and Deccan campaigns) and the Tughlaqs (Muhammad bin Tughlaq's ambitious but ultimately failed experiments — capital transfer to Daulatabad, token currency, and Doab over-taxation causing famine). Despite Firuz Shah Tughlaq's stabilization efforts, the Sultanate progressively fragmented under weaker later Tughlaq rulers, significantly shrinking in size by the century's end — setting the stage for the political fragmentation that would characterize the following period.
