Main Notes
Committees Related to Electoral Reforms
The Tarkunde Committee (1975) and the Dinesh Goswami Committee (1990) made comprehensive recommendations on electoral reforms, including state funding of elections and curbing use of money/muscle power!
The Indrajit Gupta Committee (1998) specifically examined the question of state funding of elections!
The Law Commission's 170th Report (1999) also made detailed recommendations on electoral law reform!
Electoral Reforms Before 1996
The 61st Constitutional Amendment Act, 1988 lowered the voting age from 21 to 18 years!
Electronic Voting Machines were first used experimentally in Kerala in 1982, marking the beginning of India's shift toward electronic voting!
Deployment of central paramilitary forces at sensitive polling booths was introduced to curb electoral violence and booth capturing!
Electoral Reforms of 1996
Candidate security deposits were increased to discourage frivolous candidatures!
A restriction was introduced barring candidates from contesting from more than 2 constituencies simultaneously!
Candidates were required to have a specified number of proposers for their nomination, and free supply of electoral rolls to candidates was introduced!
Electoral Reforms After 1996
EVMs were progressively made the standard mode of voting across the country from 2004 onwards!
Following the Association for Democratic Reforms case (2002) and subsequent Supreme Court directions, candidates were required, from 2003 onward, to disclose their criminal antecedents, financial assets/liabilities, and educational qualifications!
Restrictions were placed on the publication of exit poll results during ongoing polling hours to prevent influencing subsequent voting!
A ban on the sale of liquor near polling stations and around polling day was introduced to curb inducement of voters!
Electoral Reforms Since 2010
Voter Verifiable Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT) machines were introduced on a trial basis from 2013, with much wider rollout from 2017 onward, to enhance transparency and voter confidence in EVM-based voting!
The "None of the Above" (NOTA) option was introduced in 2013, following the Supreme Court's ruling in the People's Union for Civil Liberties (PUCL) case, allowing voters to formally reject all contesting candidates!
Efforts were made, at various stages, to link Aadhaar with voter ID for de-duplication of electoral rolls, though this has faced legal and implementation challenges!
The campaign silence period before polling has been progressively refined to balance campaign freedom with orderly conduct of elections!
Summary
Electoral reform recommendations have come from the Tarkunde Committee (1975), Dinesh Goswami Committee (1990), Indrajit Gupta Committee (1998, state funding), and Law Commission's 170th Report (1999). Reforms before 1996 included lowering the voting age to 18 (61st Amendment, 1988) and early EVM trials (Kerala, 1982). The 1996 reforms restricted multi-constituency contesting and increased security deposits. Post-1996 reforms introduced standardized EVM use, mandatory candidate disclosure of criminal/financial/educational background (following the ADR case), exit poll restrictions, and a liquor sale ban near polling. Since 2010, key reforms include VVPAT rollout (enhancing EVM transparency), the NOTA option (introduced via the PUCL Supreme Court judgment, 2013), and ongoing efforts around Aadhaar-voter ID linkage.
