Main Notes
Sequence of Central Asian Contacts
Following the decline of the Mauryan Empire, northwestern India experienced successive waves of Central Asian invaders — first the Indo-Greeks (Bactrian Greeks), followed by the Shakas (Scythians), then the Parthians, and finally the Kushans, who established the most significant and long-lasting of these dynasties!
The Indo-Greeks and Shakas
The Indo-Greeks ruled parts of northwestern India and Afghanistan; among their kings, Menander (also known as Milinda) is especially famous as a patron of Buddhism, immortalized in the Buddhist text Milindapanho, and this period saw the introduction of Greek-style portrait coinage!
The Shakas (Scythians) ruled parts of western and northwestern India, establishing a system of local governors known as satraps!
The Kushans and Kanishka
The Kushans were the greatest of these Central Asian dynasties to rule in India, with Kanishka as their most celebrated ruler, governing from the capital Purushapura (modern Peshawar)!
Kanishka was a significant patron of Buddhism, convening the Fourth Buddhist Council; his accession marks the beginning of the Saka Era (78 CE), which continues to be used as part of India's official national calendar!
Results of Central Asian Contact
These contacts facilitated flourishing trade along the Silk Route, connecting India with China and the Roman world!
They gave rise to the Gandhara school of art, a distinctive Greco-Buddhist fusion style blending Hellenistic artistic techniques with Buddhist religious subject matter!
Kushan rule also introduced new gold coinage and coincided with the rise of Mahayana Buddhism, in which the Buddha began to be worshipped as a deity through images and idols — distinct from the older, more austere Hinayana tradition, which did not involve idol worship!
Summary
Following Mauryan decline, northwestern India saw successive Central Asian dynasties — Indo-Greeks (notably Menander/Milinda, Buddhist patron), Shakas (satrap system), Parthians, and finally the Kushans, whose greatest ruler Kanishka (capital Purushapura) patronized Buddhism, convened the Fourth Buddhist Council, and whose accession marks the Saka Era (78 CE), still part of India's official calendar. These contacts spurred Silk Route trade linking India-China-Rome, produced the Greco-Buddhist Gandhara art school, introduced Kushan gold coinage, and coincided with the rise of image-worshipping Mahayana Buddhism, distinct from the older, idol-free Hinayana tradition.
