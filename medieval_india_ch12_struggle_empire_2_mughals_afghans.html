Main Notes
Central Asia and Babur
Babur, descended from Timurid lineage in Central Asia, sought to establish a kingdom in India after facing setbacks in his ancestral territories!
The Battle of Panipat (20 April 1526)
Babur decisively defeated Ibrahim Lodi, the last Lodi Sultan, at the First Battle of Panipat, marking the formal establishment of Mughal rule in India!
The Battle of Khanwa
Babur further consolidated his position by defeating Rana Sanga, who had led a significant Rajput confederacy against him, securing early Mughal control over North India!
The Afghans and the Significance of Babur's Advent into India
Babur's arrival introduced important new military techniques, including gunpowder weaponry and artillery, giving the Mughals a significant tactical advantage and marking the beginning of a new era in Indian political history!
Humayun's Conquest of Gujarat and his Tussle with Sher Shah
Humayun, Babur's son and successor, achieved some success such as the conquest of Gujarat, but faced a formidable challenge from the Afghan leader Sher Shah Suri!
Sher Shah and the Sur Empire (1540-55)
Sher Shah Suri decisively defeated Humayun, forcing him into exile, and established the Sur Empire, temporarily displacing Mughal rule in India!
Contribution of Sher Shah
Despite his relatively short-lived rule, Sher Shah implemented significant and lasting administrative reforms, including extensive road-building projects, introduction of the Rupiya currency, and a systematic revenue collection system that would later influence Mughal administration itself!
Summary
Babur, of Timurid Central Asian lineage, established Mughal rule in India by decisively defeating Ibrahim Lodi at the First Battle of Panipat (1526) and Rana Sanga at Khanwa, introducing significant military innovations like gunpowder artillery. However, his son Humayun was subsequently defeated by the Afghan leader Sher Shah Suri, who established the short-lived but administratively significant Sur Empire (1540-55) — introducing lasting reforms like the Rupiya currency and organized revenue systems that would later influence the restored Mughal administration.
