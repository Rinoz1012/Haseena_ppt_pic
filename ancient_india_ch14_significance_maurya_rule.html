Main Notes
Historical Significance
The Mauryan Empire was the first pan-Indian empire, controlling nearly the entire subcontinent (except the extreme south), and it established an administrative model that influenced subsequent Indian empires for centuries!
Art and Architecture
The Mauryan period produced remarkable art, particularly the polished sandstone Ashokan pillars with their distinctive animal capitals — the Sarnath Lion Capital is especially significant, having been adapted as independent India's National Emblem!
This period also saw the beginnings of rock-cut architecture, as seen in the Barabar caves!
The Role of Dhamma
Ashoka's Dhamma promoted an ethical model of governance centered on non-violence and welfare, and his missionary activities helped spread Buddhism beyond India's borders, notably to Sri Lanka and Central Asia!
Reasons for Decline
The Mauryan Empire declined due to several factors: the weak and ineffective rule of Ashoka's successors, a possible Brahmanical reaction against extensive Buddhist patronage (though this theory remains debated among historians), financial strain from maintaining a large army and extensive bureaucracy, and revolts/fragmentation in the provinces as central control weakened!
End of the Empire
The last Mauryan ruler, Brihadratha, was assassinated by his own military commander, Pushyamitra Shunga, around 185 BCE, who subsequently founded the Shunga dynasty!
Summary
The Mauryan Empire's significance lies in being India's first pan-subcontinental empire, setting an administrative template for future empires, and producing lasting art like the Ashokan pillars (the Sarnath Lion Capital became India's National Emblem) alongside early rock-cut architecture. Ashoka's Dhamma promoted ethical governance and spread Buddhism internationally via missionaries. The empire's decline stemmed from weak successors, a debated Brahmanical reaction, financial strain, and provincial fragmentation, culminating in the assassination of the last ruler Brihadratha by his commander Pushyamitra Shunga (c. 185 BCE), who founded the succeeding Shunga dynasty.
