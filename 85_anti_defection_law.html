Main Notes
Provisions of the Act
The Anti-Defection Law is contained in the 10th Schedule of the Constitution, added by the 52nd Amendment Act, 1985!
A member is disqualified from the House if they voluntarily give up membership of their political party, or if they vote or abstain from voting in the House contrary to a direction (whip) issued by their party, unless such action is condoned by the party within 15 days!
An independent member who joins a political party after election, or a nominated member who joins a political party after 6 months of being nominated, is also disqualified!
An exception exists for mergers — a member is NOT disqualified if their original political party merges with another party, and at least two-thirds of the members of that original party agree to the merger!
Deciding Authority
Questions of disqualification under the 10th Schedule are decided by the Speaker (in the Lok Sabha/State Legislative Assembly) or the Chairman (in the Rajya Sabha)!
In the Kihoto Hollohan case (1992), the Supreme Court held that while the Speaker's decision has the character of a tribunal, it is subject to judicial review by courts on limited grounds!
Evaluation of the Act
The law has curbed the earlier rampant phenomenon of political defections (popularly called "Aaya Ram Gaya Ram"), and promoted greater government stability by discouraging opportunistic floor-crossing!
However, it has been criticized for undermining an MP/MLA's freedom of expression and conscience-based voting, since members are bound to follow the party whip even on matters of personal conviction!
A significant criticism is that the Speaker, who often belongs to the ruling party, acts as the deciding authority in disqualification matters — raising concerns of conflict of interest and, in practice, resulting in considerable delays in deciding disqualification petitions!
91st Amendment Act (2003)
The 91st Amendment Act, 2003 removed the earlier exception under the 10th Schedule that had allowed a group comprising at least one-third of a party's members to "split" and escape disqualification — this loophole had been widely misused before 2003!
It also capped the total size of the Council of Ministers (at both Union and State levels) at 15% of the total strength of the concerned House, to curb the practice of offering ministerial berths as an incentive for defection!
Members disqualified under the Anti-Defection Law were barred from holding any ministerial post or remunerative political office until they are re-elected!
Summary
The Anti-Defection Law (10th Schedule, added by the 52nd Amendment, 1985) disqualifies members who voluntarily leave their party, defy a party whip (unless condoned within 15 days), or (for independents/nominated members) join a party inappropriately — with an exception for genuine mergers backed by two-thirds of the original party. The Speaker/Chairman decides disqualification questions, subject to judicial review (Kihoto Hollohan, 1992). While the law has curbed rampant floor-crossing and improved government stability, it is criticized for undermining conscience-based voting and for the Speaker's potential conflict of interest as decision-maker. The 91st Amendment Act, 2003 closed the earlier "one-third split" loophole, capped Council of Ministers size at 15% of House strength, and barred disqualified members from ministerial posts until re-elected.
