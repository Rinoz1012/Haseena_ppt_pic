Main Notes
Rise and Political Role
The Satavahanas rose to power in the Deccan region following the decline of the Mauryan Empire, filling the resulting power vacuum in this part of India!
Their capital was located at Pratishthana (modern Paithan, Maharashtra), and they were also closely associated with the Amaravati region in Andhra Pradesh!
The Satavahanas fought extensively against the Shakas (Western Kshatrapas) for control over the western Deccan region!
Trade
The Satavahanas engaged in active trade with the Roman Empire, using ports along India's western coast to export spices, textiles, and gems!
Roman gold coins discovered at Satavahana archaeological sites provide clear evidence of this flourishing Indo-Roman trade relationship!
Religious Patronage
The Satavahanas patronized both Brahmanical Hinduism and Buddhism, and their support was instrumental in the construction of important Buddhist stupas, most notably the famous Amaravati stupa!
The Beginning of Land Grants
The Satavahanas were among the earliest Indian dynasties to grant land — often tax-free — to Brahmanas and religious institutions!
This practice would expand considerably in later centuries, particularly from the Gupta period onward, and is considered an early sign of the feudal-type land relations that would characterize much of subsequent Indian history!
Administrative Structure
The Satavahana king was assisted by various officials in administering the kingdom, with some administrative features possibly showing influence from the earlier Mauryan administrative model!
Summary
The Satavahanas rose in the Deccan after Mauryan decline, ruling from Pratishthana and associated with Amaravati, and fought the Shakas for control of the western Deccan. They engaged in flourishing trade with Rome via western coast ports (evidenced by Roman gold coin finds), patronized both Hinduism and Buddhism (notably the Amaravati stupa), and were among the earliest Indian dynasties to grant tax-free land to Brahmanas and religious institutions — an early seed of the feudal-type land relations that would expand significantly from the Gupta period onward.
