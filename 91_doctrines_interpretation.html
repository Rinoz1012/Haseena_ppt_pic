91. Doctrines of Constitutional Interpretation — Cornell Notes 06 Jul 2026
Keywords

PYQ Alert
Waiver doctrine = Indian exception(FRs can't be waived)! Prospective overruling FIRST applied in Golak Nath(1967)! Pith & Substance vs Colourable Legislation = often confused, opposite outcomes!
Main Notes
Doctrine of Severability
When a part of a statute is unconstitutional, only that offending part is struck down, provided it can be separated from the rest of the law without affecting its overall scheme — if inseparable, the entire law falls!
Doctrine of Waiver
Unlike many ordinary legal rights, Fundamental Rights CANNOT be waived by an individual, since they exist not merely for the individual's benefit but for the larger public good and the maintenance of a healthy constitutional order!
Doctrine of Eclipse
A pre-Constitution law inconsistent with Fundamental Rights is not void ab initio — it merely becomes unenforceable ("eclipsed") against citizens; it can become operative again if the relevant FR is later amended to remove the inconsistency!
Doctrine of Territorial Nexus
A state law can validly apply to persons, property, objects, or transactions located outside the state's territory, provided there exists a sufficient nexus/connection between the state and the matter being regulated!
Doctrine of Pith and Substance
When examining a law's constitutional validity, courts look at its "pith and substance" (true nature and character) — if the true subject-matter falls within the enacting legislature's competence, mere incidental encroachment into a field reserved for another legislature does not invalidate the law!
Doctrine of Colourable Legislation
Based on the principle "what cannot be done directly cannot be done indirectly" — a legislature cannot, under the guise or disguise of exercising a power it possesses, enact a law that in substance falls outside its competence!
Doctrine of Implied Powers
Powers not expressly conferred by the Constitution or a statute, but reasonably necessary and incidental to effectively exercise an expressly granted power, are presumed to be implicitly available!
Doctrine of Incidental and Ancillary Powers
The power to legislate on a particular subject includes the power to legislate on matters that are incidental or ancillary to that subject, even if such matters are not separately/explicitly enumerated!
Doctrine of Precedent
Under Art 141, the law declared by the Supreme Court is binding on all courts within India, embodying the principle of stare decisis (adherence to precedent) to ensure consistency and predictability in judicial decision-making!
Doctrine of Occupied Field
Under Art 254, if Parliament has legislated on a matter falling within the Concurrent List, a State law on the same matter becomes void to the extent of any repugnancy/conflict with the central law, since Parliament's law is deemed to have "occupied the field"!
Doctrine of Prospective Overruling
A court's new interpretation of law applies only prospectively (to future cases), and not retrospectively to past transactions already settled under the earlier interpretation — first applied by the Supreme Court in the I.C. Golak Nath case (1967)!
Doctrine of Harmonious Construction
When two or more provisions of the Constitution appear to conflict, courts must interpret them in a manner that gives effect to both provisions as far as possible, rather than treating one as overriding or nullifying the other!
Doctrine of Liberal Interpretation
Constitutional provisions, especially those relating to Fundamental Rights, are to be interpreted broadly and generously, in a manner that gives full effect to their underlying purpose, rather than through a narrow, restrictive reading!
Summary
These interpretive doctrines guide how courts read the Constitution and resolve legislative disputes: Severability and Eclipse deal with partially/temporarily invalid laws; Waiver uniquely bars individuals from giving up Fundamental Rights; Territorial Nexus, Pith and Substance, Colourable Legislation, Implied Powers, and Incidental/Ancillary Powers all govern the validity and scope of legislative competence; Precedent and Occupied Field maintain consistency and resolve Centre-State legislative conflicts; and Prospective Overruling, Harmonious Construction, and Liberal Interpretation guide how courts apply new rulings and reconcile or expand constitutional meaning.
