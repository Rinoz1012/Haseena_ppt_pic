Main Notes
Immediate Trigger
The revolt's immediate spark was the controversy over greased cartridges for the new Enfield rifle, which sepoys believed (rightly or through rumor) were greased with cow and pig fat, offending both Hindu and Muslim religious sensibilities!
Broader Underlying Causes
Beyond this immediate trigger, deeper causes included political grievances (resentment over annexations and the Doctrine of Lapse), economic hardships (heavy taxation and decline of traditional Indian industries under British policies), social and religious anxieties (fears of forced conversion to Christianity), and specific military grievances among sepoys regarding pay and service conditions!
Key Centers of Revolt
The revolt began at Meerut and quickly spread to become significant at several other key centers, including Delhi, Kanpur, Lucknow, and Jhansi!
Key Leaders
Bahadur Shah II, the aging Mughal emperor, served as a symbolic figurehead for the revolt in Delhi; Rani Lakshmibai led resistance at Jhansi; Nana Sahib led the uprising at Kanpur, supported by Tantia Tope; and Begum Hazrat Mahal led resistance at Lucknow!
Nature and Extent of the Revolt
While widespread and significant, the revolt did not constitute a fully unified pan-Indian rebellion — several regions and Indian rulers remained loyal to the British, and the revolt's intensity and character varied considerably across different parts of India!
Suppression and Aftermath
The British ultimately suppressed the revolt by 1858, an outcome that led to major administrative restructuring — the East India Company's rule was formally ended, and India came under direct rule of the British Crown!
Summary
The Revolt of 1857 was immediately triggered by the greased cartridges controversy, but was rooted in deeper political, economic, social/religious, and military grievances against British rule. It centered on key locations including Meerut, Delhi, Kanpur, Lucknow, and Jhansi, led by figures such as Bahadur Shah II, Rani Lakshmibai, Nana Sahib, Tantia Tope, and Begum Hazrat Mahal. Though widespread, it was not a fully unified pan-Indian uprising, with several regions remaining loyal to the British. Its suppression by 1858 led to a fundamental restructuring of British governance in India — ending East India Company rule and establishing direct British Crown rule, a pivotal turning point in Indian colonial history.
