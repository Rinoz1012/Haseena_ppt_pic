Main Notes
Appointment & Term
Art 148 provides for a Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG), appointed by the President!
Term = 6 years or till 65 years of age, whichever is earlier; can resign anytime by writing to the President!
Independence
Removed only in the same manner as a Supreme Court judge (impeachment by Parliament on grounds of proved misbehaviour or incapacity)!
Not eligible for further office under the Government of India or any State after ceasing to hold the CAG post!
Service conditions cannot be varied to their disadvantage after appointment!
Salary and administrative expenses of CAG's office charged on the Consolidated Fund of India — not subject to Parliament's annual vote!
Duties & Powers
Art 149 empowers Parliament to prescribe CAG's duties/powers relating to audit of accounts of the Union, States, and other authorities/bodies!
Audits all expenditure from the Consolidated Fund of India, States, and UTs with legislatures!
Compiles and maintains accounts of certain states, on their request!
Submits audit reports relating to Union accounts to the President, who lays them before Parliament; reports on State accounts go to the Governor, who lays them before the State Legislature!
Role
Regarded as the "guardian of the public purse" — ensures financial accountability of the executive to the legislature!
Functions primarily as a POST-audit body — examines expenditure after it has been incurred, rather than approving it beforehand!
CAG and Corporations
Departmental commercial undertakings — subject to FULL audit by CAG, like any other government department!
Statutory corporations — extent of CAG's audit depends on provisions of the specific Act that created the corporation; varies from full audit to no audit!
Government companies (under Companies Act) — audited by private chartered accountants appointed by CAG, with CAG conducting a supplementary/test audit!
Appleby's Criticism
Paul H. Appleby, an American public administration expert who reviewed Indian administrative structures in the 1950s, criticized CAG's then-existing pre-audit (pre-check) function!
He argued that extensive pre-audit checks caused unnecessary administrative delays and hampered efficient governance, recommending that CAG's role be limited mainly to post-audit!
Challenges
Faces resource and manpower constraints relative to the scale of government expenditure it must audit!
Audit reports are often delayed, reducing their timeliness and impact!
Limited mandate over Public-Private Partnership (PPP) projects and private entities receiving government funds!
Has no power to penalize or enforce corrective action — can only report irregularities for Parliament/Legislature to act upon!
Summary
CAG (Art 148-151) is appointed by the President, term 6 yrs/65 yrs, removable only like a SC judge, with independence safeguarded via a post-retirement employment bar, protected service conditions, and expenditure charged on the Consolidated Fund. Duties (Art 149) = auditing Union/State accounts, submitting reports routed through President→Parliament or Governor→State Legislature; role = post-audit guardian of the public purse ensuring executive accountability. CAG's audit of corporations varies: full for departmental undertakings, Act-dependent for statutory corporations, and supplementary-only for government companies (primary audit by CAG-appointed private auditors). Appleby criticized CAG's earlier pre-audit function as delay-causing, recommending a post-audit focus. Challenges include resource constraints, delayed reports, limited PPP oversight, and lack of penalizing power.
