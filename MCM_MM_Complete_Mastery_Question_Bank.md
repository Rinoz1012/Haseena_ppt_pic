### LEVEL 1 — BASIC / FOUNDATION

**Q1. [1M, MCQ]** SI unit of magnetic field is:
(a) Tesla (b) Weber (c) Gauss (d) Henry
**Answer:** (a) Tesla
**Concept:** $B = F/qv\sin\theta$; unit is $\text{N s C}^{-1}\text{m}^{-1}$ = Tesla (T). 1 T = 10⁴ Gauss.
**NCERT Topic:** Magnetic field basics (Ch.4, Intro)
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Confusing Tesla with Gauss or Weber (Weber is unit of magnetic flux).

**Q2. [1M]** Write the expression for the magnetic force on a charge $q$ moving with velocity $\vec v$ in a magnetic field $\vec B$.
**Answer:** $\vec F = q(\vec v \times \vec B)$
**Concept:** Force is perpendicular to both $\vec v$ and $\vec B$; magnitude $qvB\sin\theta$.
**NCERT Topic:** Magnetic force (4.2)
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Writing scalar product instead of cross product.

**Q3. [1M]** A charged particle moves parallel to a magnetic field. What is the force on it?
**Answer:** Zero, since $\theta = 0°$ or $180°$, so $\sin\theta = 0$.
**Concept:** Direct application of $F = qvB\sin\theta$.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Students think force is maximum in all cases; forget angle dependence.

**Q4. [1M]** State Biot–Savart law.
**Answer:** $d\vec B = \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\dfrac{I\,d\vec l \times \hat r}{r^2}$ — magnetic field due to a small current element is proportional to $I\,dl\sin\theta$ and inversely proportional to $r^2$.
**Concept:** Analogous to Coulomb's law but for magnetic sources; always has $\sin\theta$ term.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.3
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Forgetting the $\sin\theta$ factor; confusing with Ampere's law.

**Q5. [1M]** What is the value of $\mu_0$ (permeability of free space)?
**Answer:** $4\pi \times 10^{-7} \text{ T m A}^{-1}$
**Concept:** Fundamental constant appearing in Biot–Savart and Ampere's law.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.3
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Confusing with $\varepsilon_0$ or forgetting the $4\pi$ factor.

**Q6. [1M]** State Ampere's Circuital Law.
**Answer:** $\oint \vec B \cdot d\vec l = \mu_0 I_{enc}$ — line integral of B around a closed loop equals $\mu_0$ times current enclosed.
**Concept:** Magnetic analogue of Gauss's law for a closed path (Amperian loop).
**NCERT Topic:** 4.4
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Using total current instead of only enclosed current.

**Q7. [1M]** What is a solenoid?
**Answer:** A long, closely wound helical coil of wire; behaves like a bar magnet producing a fairly uniform field inside.
**Concept:** Basis for deriving $B = \mu_0 n I$.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.4
**Freq:** FA

**Q8. [1M]** Define magnetic dipole moment of a current loop.
**Answer:** $m = NIA$, direction given by right-hand rule (perpendicular to loop plane).
**Concept:** Analogous to electric dipole moment $p = q \times 2a$.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.6
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Forgetting factor $N$ (number of turns).

**Q9. [1M]** What is the function of a radial magnetic field in a moving coil galvanometer?
**Answer:** Keeps the plane of the coil always parallel to $B$, so torque is constant $(\tau = NIAB)$ for all deflections, giving a linear scale.
**Concept:** Without radial field, $\tau \propto \cos\theta$, giving non-uniform scale.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.7
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Saying it "increases the deflection" rather than explaining linearity.

**Q10. [1M]** Define one ampere using force between two parallel wires.
**Answer:** 1 A is that current which, when flowing in each of two infinitely long, straight, parallel conductors placed 1 m apart in vacuum, produces a force of $2\times10^{-7}$ N per metre of length between them.
**Concept:** SI base unit definition via force formula $F/l = \mu_0 I_1 I_2/2\pi d$.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.5
**Freq:** IMP

**Q11. [1M]** What is the SI unit of magnetic dipole moment?
**Answer:** A m² (ampere-metre²), also J/T.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.6
**Freq:** IMP

**Q12. [1M]** A bar magnet is equivalent to a solenoid carrying current. True or False? Justify in one line.
**Answer:** True — both produce similar field patterns (dipole field) at large distances; a solenoid's field lines look identical to those of a bar magnet.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.2
**Freq:** FA

**Q13. [1M]** Define magnetic susceptibility.
**Answer:** $\chi = M/H$, ratio of intensity of magnetisation to magnetising field intensity; measures how easily a material gets magnetised.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.7
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Confusing susceptibility ($\chi$) with permeability ($\mu$).

**Q14. [1M]** Name one diamagnetic, one paramagnetic, and one ferromagnetic substance.
**Answer:** Diamagnetic – Bismuth/Copper; Paramagnetic – Aluminium/Sodium; Ferromagnetic – Iron/Cobalt/Nickel.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.8
**Freq:** FA

**Q15. [1M]** What is angle of dip?
**Answer:** The angle that the Earth's total magnetic field makes with the horizontal at a place.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.6
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Confusing dip with declination.

### LEVEL 2 — EASY

**Q16. [2M]** A proton and an electron enter a uniform magnetic field with the same velocity, perpendicular to $B$. Compare the radii of their circular paths.
**Answer:** $r = mv/qB$. Since charge magnitude is equal, $r_p/r_e = m_p/m_e \approx 1836$. Proton's radius is much larger.
**Concept:** Radius directly proportional to mass, inversely to charge.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2 (circular motion in B field)
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Forgetting that direction of circulation is opposite for opposite charges, though question here asks only about radius.

**Q17. [2M]** Derive the expression for cyclotron frequency and show it is independent of speed.
**Answer:** For circular motion, $qvB = mv^2/r \Rightarrow r = mv/qB$. Period $T = 2\pi r/v = 2\pi m/qB$. Frequency $\nu = qB/2\pi m$ — depends only on $q$, $B$, $m$; independent of $v$ and $r$.
**Concept:** This independence is the working principle of the cyclotron.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2.1 (Cyclotron)
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Thinking frequency depends on speed since radius increases with speed — but the two effects cancel.

**Q18. [2M]** State the principle and one limitation of a cyclotron.
**Answer:** Principle: A charged particle can be accelerated to high energies using a relatively small alternating voltage by making it cross the same electric field again and again with the help of a magnetic field. Limitation: Cannot accelerate electrons (too light, become relativistic quickly) or neutral particles; fails at relativistic speeds since frequency of revolution no longer matches applied AC frequency.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2.1
**Freq:** FA

**Q19. [2M]** Two long straight parallel conductors carry currents in the same direction. Show whether they attract or repel, with reasoning.
**Answer:** They attract. Current-carrying conductor 1 produces field $B_1$ circling it; at the location of conductor 2 (same direction current), using $F = I_2 \vec l \times \vec B_1$ and right-hand rule, force on conductor 2 is directed towards conductor 1. By Newton's third law, force on 1 is towards 2. Hence like currents attract (opposite to electrostatic like charges).
**NCERT Topic:** 4.5
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Applying the electrostatics "like repels like" intuition incorrectly.

**Q20. [2M]** A circular coil of radius $R$ carrying current $I$ has magnetic moment $m = I\pi R^2$. If the same wire is bent into a square, find new magnetic moment (same current, same total wire length).
**Answer:** Circumference $2\pi R = 4a \Rightarrow a = \pi R/2$. New moment $m' = I a^2 = I\pi^2R^2/4$. Ratio $m'/m = \pi/4 \approx 0.785$; square coil has smaller moment than circular coil for the same wire length.
**Concept:** For fixed perimeter, circle encloses maximum area → circular coil gives max magnetic moment.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.6 (numerical extension)
**Freq:** IMP
**Common mistake:** Forgetting to keep total wire length (perimeter) equal, using same radius instead.

**Q21. [2M]** Why is the core of a moving coil galvanometer made of soft iron?
**Answer:** Soft iron has high permeability, concentrating the magnetic field and making it radial and strong, thus increasing sensitivity of the galvanometer; also, soft iron is not permanently magnetised (low retentivity) so field configuration stays consistent.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.7
**Freq:** FA

**Q22. [2M]** Why can't a magnetic field alone accelerate a charged particle (i.e., increase its kinetic energy)?
**Answer:** $\vec F = q(\vec v \times \vec B)$ is always perpendicular to $\vec v$, so it does no work ($W = \vec F \cdot \vec d = 0$). It changes direction of motion, not speed, hence KE remains constant.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Confusing "force acts" with "work is done."

**Q23. [2M]** A toroid has $N$ turns and mean radius $r$, carrying current $I$. Give expression for field inside and explain why field outside (and in the hollow interior of the toroid) is zero.
**Answer:** $B = \mu_0 N I / 2\pi r$ inside the core. Outside and in the central hollow region, an Amperian loop encloses zero net current (loops don't enclose the coil turns properly — each turn's current cancels for external/central loops), so $B = 0$.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.4.2
**Freq:** FA

**Q24. [2M]** State Gauss's Law in magnetism and explain its physical significance.
**Answer:** $\oint \vec B \cdot d\vec A = 0$ over any closed surface. Significance: magnetic monopoles do not exist; magnetic field lines always form closed loops (no isolated source/sink like electric charges).
**NCERT Topic:** 5.5
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Writing it as equal to $\mu_0 q_m$ (there is no magnetic charge $q_m$ in reality).

**Q25. [2M]** Distinguish between diamagnetic and paramagnetic materials on the basis of behaviour in a non-uniform magnetic field.
**Answer:** Diamagnetic materials are weakly repelled (move from stronger to weaker field region); paramagnetic materials are weakly attracted (move from weaker to stronger field region), because induced magnetisation is opposite to field in diamagnetics and along the field in paramagnetics.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.8
**Freq:** FA

**Q26. [2M]** What are the three elements of Earth's magnetism? Define any one.
**Answer:** Declination ($\theta$), Angle of Dip ($\delta$), Horizontal component ($H$). Declination: angle between geographic meridian and magnetic meridian at a place.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.6
**Freq:** FA

### LEVEL 3 — MEDIUM

**Q27. [3M, Derivation]** Derive the expression for magnetic field at the centre of a circular current-carrying loop using Biot–Savart law.
**Answer:**
Using Biot-Savart: $dB = \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\dfrac{I\,dl \sin 90°}{R^2}$ (since $d\vec l \perp \hat r$ at centre for every element).
Integrating around full loop: $B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi R^2}\oint dl = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi R^2}(2\pi R) = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2R}$.
For $N$ turns: $B = \dfrac{\mu_0 N I}{2R}$.
**Concept:** Every element contributes field in the same direction (perpendicular to plane) at centre, so scalar addition works.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.3.1
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Forgetting $\sin\theta = 1$ is valid ONLY at the centre, not at axial points.

**Q28. [3M, Derivation]** Using Ampere's law, derive the magnetic field due to a long straight current-carrying conductor at a point at perpendicular distance $r$.
**Answer:** Choose a circular Amperian loop of radius $r$ centred on the wire, in a plane perpendicular to it. By symmetry $B$ is constant in magnitude and tangential everywhere on this loop.
$\oint \vec B \cdot d\vec l = B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 I \Rightarrow B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}$
**Concept:** Symmetry argument crucial for pulling $B$ out of the integral.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.4.1
**Freq:** FA (appears almost every year in some form)
**Common mistake:** Choosing a non-circular/asymmetric loop, making $B$ non-constant along the path.

**Q29. [3M]** A current of 10 A flows through a long straight wire. Calculate the magnetic field at a point 20 cm from it. Also find the field if the distance is doubled.
**Answer:** $B = \mu_0 I/2\pi r = (4\pi\times10^{-7}\times10)/(2\pi\times0.2) = 10^{-5}$ T $= 1\times10^{-5}$ T.
At double distance ($r=0.4$m): $B \propto 1/r$, so $B' = 0.5\times10^{-5}$ T $= 5\times10^{-6}$ T.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.4.1 (numerical)
**Freq:** FA

**Q30. [3M]** Two parallel wires carry currents 5 A and 8 A in opposite directions, separated by 4 cm. Find the force per unit length between them and state its nature.
**Answer:** $F/l = \mu_0 I_1 I_2/2\pi d = (4\pi\times10^{-7}\times5\times8)/(2\pi\times0.04) = 2\times10^{-4}$ N/m.
Nature: repulsive (opposite currents repel).
**NCERT Topic:** 4.5
**Freq:** FA

**Q31. [3M]** Derive expression for torque on a rectangular current loop placed in a uniform magnetic field, with the plane of the loop parallel to $B$.
**Answer:** Consider loop of sides $a$ (length) and $b$ (breadth), current $I$, in field $B$ with plane parallel to $B$ (so normal $\perp B$). Forces on sides of length $b$ (parallel to B) are along the wire — zero net effect on rotation directly, but forces on sides of length $a$ are $F = BIa$, equal & opposite, separated by $b$, forming a couple.
Torque $\tau = F \times b = BIa \times b = BIA$ (A = ab).
For $N$ turns: $\tau = NIAB = mB$ (since $m = NIA$), and in general $\tau = \vec m \times \vec B$, magnitude $mB\sin\theta$.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.6
**Freq:** FA (very frequently asked derivation)
**Common mistake:** Not identifying which pair of sides contributes zero torque and which pair forms the couple.

**Q32. [3M]** A rectangular loop of sides 10 cm × 5 cm carries a current of 12 A. It is placed with its plane making 30° with a magnetic field of 0.8 T. Find torque on the loop.
**Answer:** $A = 0.1\times0.05 = 5\times10^{-3}$ m². Angle between $B$ and plane = 30°, so angle between $B$ and normal $\theta = 60°$.
$\tau = NIAB\sin\theta = 1\times12\times5\times10^{-3}\times0.8\times\sin60° = 0.048\times0.866 = 0.0416$ N·m.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.6 numerical
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Using angle with plane instead of angle with normal in the $\sin\theta$ formula — this is the single most common numerical error in this chapter.

**Q33. [3M]** Explain construction and working of a moving coil galvanometer with a labelled diagram description.
**Answer:** Consists of a rectangular coil of many turns wound on a light aluminium frame, suspended between concave poles of a permanent horseshoe magnet with a soft-iron cylindrical core inside (creates radial field). When current $I$ flows, torque $\tau = NIAB$ acts on coil, coil rotates till restoring torque of spring $k\phi$ balances it: $NIAB = k\phi \Rightarrow I = (k/NAB)\phi$. Deflection $\phi \propto I$, giving a linear scale.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.7
**Freq:** FA

**Q34. [3M]** Define current sensitivity and voltage sensitivity of a galvanometer. Show that increasing current sensitivity does not necessarily increase voltage sensitivity.
**Answer:** Current sensitivity $I_s = \phi/I = NAB/k$. Voltage sensitivity $V_s = \phi/V = NAB/kR$ (R = coil resistance).
If we increase sensitivity by increasing $N$, resistance $R$ also increases proportionally (more wire), so $V_s = NAB/k(N r_0)$ may remain unchanged if $R \propto N$. Hence higher current sensitivity doesn't guarantee higher voltage sensitivity.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.7
**Freq:** IMP (conceptual, commonly tested)
**Common mistake:** Assuming the two sensitivities always increase together.

**Q35. [3M]** Derive the expression for magnetic field on the axis of a circular current loop at distance $x$ from centre.
**Answer:** By Biot-Savart, each element contributes $dB$ at angle; components perpendicular to axis cancel by symmetry, axial components add:
$B = \dfrac{\mu_0 I R^2}{2(R^2+x^2)^{3/2}}$ (for N turns, multiply by N).
**NCERT Topic:** 4.3.2
**Freq:** FA

**Q36. [3M]** A magnet of magnetic moment $M$ is placed in a uniform field $B$ making angle $\theta$. Derive expression for potential energy of the magnetic dipole and find the position of stable and unstable equilibrium.
**Answer:** $U(\theta) = -MB\cos\theta$ (taking reference at $\theta=90°$ as zero, or $U = -\vec M\cdot\vec B$).
Stable equilibrium at $\theta = 0°$ ($U_{min} = -MB$, dipole aligned with field).
Unstable equilibrium at $\theta = 180°$ ($U_{max} = +MB$).
**NCERT Topic:** 5.3
**Freq:** FA

**Q37. [3M]** Why is it that soft iron is used for making electromagnets while steel is preferred for permanent magnets?
**Answer:** Soft iron has high permeability, low retentivity and low coercivity — it magnetises and demagnetises easily, essential for electromagnets which must switch on/off quickly. Steel has high retentivity and high coercivity — retains magnetism strongly, resisting external fields that would demagnetise it, so it's ideal for permanent magnets.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.9
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Reversing the logic (saying steel is used for electromagnets).

**Q38. [3M, Case-based intro type]** Explain the concept of hysteresis in ferromagnetic materials with reference to a B-H curve (describe qualitatively).
**Answer:** As H is increased from zero, B increases along the initial magnetisation curve and saturates. On decreasing H to zero, B does not return to zero — it retains a value called retentivity ($B_r$). To bring B to zero, a reverse field called coercivity ($H_c$) must be applied. Cycling H produces a closed loop (hysteresis loop); the area of the loop represents energy dissipated as heat per cycle.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.8 (ferromagnetism, hysteresis — exemplar extension)
**Freq:** IMP

### LEVEL 4 — HARD

**Q39. [3M]** A charged particle enters a region with perpendicular electric and magnetic fields (velocity selector) and passes through undeflected. Derive the condition and explain what happens if only $B$ is switched off.
**Answer:** For undeflected motion, electric force balances magnetic force: $qE = qvB \Rightarrow v = E/B$.
If $B$ is switched off, only electric force $qE$ acts, so the particle accelerates in the direction of $E$ (deviates from straight path, follows a parabolic trajectory as in a CRT).
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2 (Motion in combined E and B fields)
**Freq:** FA
**Common mistake:** Forgetting direction consideration — forces must be exactly opposite, not just equal magnitude for any orientation.

**Q40. [5M, Long/Case]** Derive the expression for the magnetic field inside a long solenoid using Ampere's Circuital Law. State the assumptions made.
**Answer:**
Assumption: solenoid is long (length >> diameter) so field outside is negligible and field inside is uniform, parallel to axis.
Consider rectangular Amperian loop PQRS: PQ (length $L$) inside solenoid parallel to axis, RS outside (field ~0), QR & SP perpendicular to field (contribute zero as $B\perp dl$).
$\oint B\cdot dl = BL$ (only PQ contributes)
Current enclosed = $n L \times I$ (n = turns per unit length)
$BL = \mu_0 n L I \Rightarrow B = \mu_0 n I$
Independent of position inside (as long as away from the ends) and independent of solenoid's cross-sectional radius.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.4.1
**Freq:** FA — appears almost every year, one of the highest-value derivations
**Common mistake:** Not justifying why RS, QR, SP contribute zero; sloppy Amperian loop construction.

**Q41. [3M]** An electron moving with speed $2\times10^6$ m/s enters a magnetic field of 0.5 T perpendicular to it. Find (a) radius of circular path (b) time period (c) kinetic energy of electron. ($m_e = 9.1\times10^{-31}$ kg, $e=1.6\times10^{-19}$ C)
**Answer:**
(a) $r = mv/eB = (9.1\times10^{-31}\times2\times10^6)/(1.6\times10^{-19}\times0.5) = 2.275\times10^{-5}$ m
(b) $T = 2\pi m/eB = 2\pi\times9.1\times10^{-31}/(1.6\times10^{-19}\times0.5) = 7.14\times10^{-11}$ s
(c) KE $= \frac12 mv^2 = 0.5\times9.1\times10^{-31}\times4\times10^{12} = 1.82\times10^{-18}$ J $\approx 11.4$ eV
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2 numerical
**Freq:** FA

**Q42. [3M]** A solenoid of length 1 m and 5000 turns carries a current of 3 A. Find the magnetic field at the centre (well inside) and compare with Earth's magnetic field ($\approx 4\times10^{-5}$T).
**Answer:** $n = 5000/1 = 5000$ turns/m. $B = \mu_0 n I = 4\pi\times10^{-7}\times5000\times3 = 1.885\times10^{-2}$ T.
Ratio to Earth's field $= 1.885\times10^{-2}/4\times10^{-5} \approx 471$ times stronger.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.4.1 numerical
**Freq:** FA

**Q43. [5M, Derivation + application]** A bar magnet of length $2l$ and pole strength $m$ is placed with centre at origin along x-axis. Derive expression for the magnetic field at (a) an axial point at distance $d$ and (b) an equatorial point at distance $d$ from centre, and hence show $B_{axial} = 2B_{equatorial}$ for short magnet ($d>>l$).
**Answer:**
Axial: $B_{axial} = \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\dfrac{2Md}{(d^2-l^2)^2} \approx \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\dfrac{2M}{d^3}$ for $d>>l$ (where $M = m\times2l$)
Equatorial: $B_{eq} = \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\dfrac{M}{(d^2+l^2)^{3/2}} \approx \dfrac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\dfrac{M}{d^3}$ for $d>>l$
Ratio: $B_{axial}/B_{eq} = 2$.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.2, 5.3
**Freq:** FA — extremely important derivation, asked almost every year in some form
**Common mistake:** Forgetting the short-magnet approximation step; mixing up which formula is axial vs equatorial.

**Q44. [3M, HOTS]** Two identical bar magnets are placed such that their axes are perpendicular to each other, one along the axial line and the other along equatorial line of a test point P equidistant from both. If resultant field makes 45° with one magnet's axis, what can you conclude about their magnetic moments?
**Answer:** Since resultant makes 45° with axial-magnet's field direction, the two component fields must be equal in magnitude: $B_{axial,1} = B_{eq,2}$. Since $B_{axial} = 2B_{eq}$ for identical distance, we need $\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{2M_1}{d^3} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{M_2}{d^3} \Rightarrow M_2 = 2M_1$. So the magnet contributing the equatorial field must have twice the magnetic moment.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.3 (applied HOTS)
**Freq:** RBP (Olympiad-adjacent but syllabus-consistent)

**Q45. [3M]** Show mathematically why the magnetic field due to an infinite current sheet (conceptual extension) or use Ampere's law to justify why field just outside a long straight wire's surface only depends on enclosed current, not on the current distribution's exact shape.
**Answer:** By Ampere's law, $\oint B\cdot dl = \mu_0 I_{enc}$ depends only on the total enclosed current, regardless of how it is distributed within the loop (thin wire vs thick cylindrical conductor of same total current), as long as symmetric current distribution is assumed for B to be computed simply. This is analogous to Gauss's law depending only on enclosed charge.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.4 (conceptual insight)
**Freq:** IMP

**Q46. [3M]** A galvanometer with resistance 50 Ω gives full-scale deflection for a current of 5 mA. Convert it into (a) an ammeter of range 5 A (b) a voltmeter of range 10 V.
**Answer:**
(a) Shunt $S$: $I_g G = (I-I_g)S \Rightarrow S = \dfrac{I_g G}{I-I_g} = \dfrac{5\times10^{-3}\times50}{5-0.005} \approx 0.05$ Ω (connect in parallel).
(b) Series resistance $R$: $V = I_g(G+R) \Rightarrow R = V/I_g - G = 10/(5\times10^{-3}) - 50 = 2000-50 = 1950$ Ω (connect in series).
**NCERT Topic:** 4.7 (Ammeter/voltmeter conversion)
**Freq:** FA — one of the most frequently repeated numerical types

**Q47. [3M]** Explain why an ammeter is always connected in series and must have very low resistance, while a voltmeter is connected in parallel and must have very high resistance.
**Answer:** Ammeter measures current, so it must not alter the current in the circuit — needs very low resistance to minimize voltage drop across it (series connection carries full circuit current). Voltmeter measures potential difference across a component without drawing significant current from it — needs very high resistance so it draws negligible current, hence connected in parallel.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.7
**Freq:** FA

### LEVEL 5 — BOARD-LEVEL DIFFICULT

**Q48. [5M, Case-Based]**
*Read the passage and answer:*
A cyclotron is used to accelerate protons. The magnetic field used is 1.5 T, and the oscillator frequency is such that it matches the cyclotron frequency of protons. The radius of the dees is 0.5 m. ($m_p = 1.67\times10^{-27}$ kg, $q=1.6\times10^{-19}$ C)

(i) Find the cyclotron frequency. [1]
(ii) Find the maximum velocity attained by the proton. [1]
(iii) Find the maximum kinetic energy gained (in MeV). [2]
(iv) Why can this device not be used to accelerate electrons? [1]

**Answers:**
(i) $\nu = qB/2\pi m = (1.6\times10^{-19}\times1.5)/(2\pi\times1.67\times10^{-27}) \approx 2.29\times10^7$ Hz
(ii) $v_{max} = qBR/m = (1.6\times10^{-19}\times1.5\times0.5)/1.67\times10^{-27} \approx 7.19\times10^7$ m/s
(iii) $KE = \frac12mv^2 = 0.5\times1.67\times10^{-27}\times(7.19\times10^7)^2 \approx 4.32\times10^{-12}$ J $\approx 27$ MeV
(iv) Electrons are too light; they quickly reach relativistic speeds, causing their cyclotron frequency to fall out of sync with the fixed oscillator frequency, so they cannot be efficiently accelerated.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2.1 (Case-based application)
**Freq:** FA (case-based cyclotron questions are a favorite pattern)

**Q49. [5M, Derivation]** State and derive Ampere's Circuital law application to find field due to a toroid, and explain why the field in the empty space inside the toroid (the "donut hole") and outside the toroid is zero. Support with 3 separate Amperian loop arguments.
**Answer:**
- Loop 1 (inside the hollow interior, radius $r<r_1$): encloses zero current $\Rightarrow B_1 = 0$
- Loop 2 (inside the toroid core, radius $r_1<r<r_2$): encloses current $NI$ $\Rightarrow B(2\pi r) = \mu_0 NI \Rightarrow B = \mu_0NI/2\pi r$
- Loop 3 (outside toroid, $r>r_2$): each turn crosses the loop's area twice, once in and once out, net enclosed current = 0 $\Rightarrow B_3 = 0$
**NCERT Topic:** 4.4.2
**Freq:** FA — a very common 5-mark derivation, often combined with solenoid comparison

**Q50. [5M]** Derive the expression for force per unit length between two long parallel current-carrying conductors and use it to define the SI unit "ampere." Also discuss the nature of the force (attractive/repulsive) for like and unlike currents.
**Answer:**
Wire 1 carrying $I_1$ produces $B_1 = \mu_0I_1/2\pi d$ at wire 2's location.
Force on length $l$ of wire 2: $F = B_1 I_2 l = \dfrac{\mu_0 I_1I_2 l}{2\pi d}$
$\Rightarrow F/l = \dfrac{\mu_0 I_1I_2}{2\pi d}$
For $I_1=I_2=1$A, $d=1$m: $F/l = 2\times10^{-7}$ N/m — this defines 1 ampere.
Nature: parallel (same direction) currents attract; antiparallel (opposite direction) currents repel.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.5
**Freq:** FA — extremely important, definition-based derivation

**Q51. [3M, Assertion–Reason]**
**Assertion (A):** A magnetic field does not exert force on a stationary charge.
**Reason (R):** Magnetic force depends on the velocity of the charge, and for $v=0$, $F=qvB\sin\theta = 0$.
Options: (a) Both A and R true, R is correct explanation of A (b) Both true, R not correct explanation (c) A true, R false (d) A false, R true
**Answer:** (a)
**Concept:** Direct consequence of Lorentz force law.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2
**Freq:** FA (A-R format is a staple pattern in CBSE)

**Q52. [3M, Assertion–Reason]**
**Assertion (A):** Ferromagnetic materials lose their magnetism above the Curie temperature.
**Reason (R):** Above Curie temperature, thermal agitation destroys the alignment of magnetic domains, and the material behaves like a paramagnetic substance.
**Answer:** (a) Both true and R correctly explains A.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.8
**Freq:** IMP

**Q53. [5M, HOTS + Numerical]** A rectangular loop carrying current $I$ is placed near an infinite straight current-carrying wire, with one side parallel to the wire at distance $a$ and the far side at distance $b$ (loop lies in the plane of the wire). Derive expression for net force on the loop.
**Answer:**
Near side (distance $a$): attractive force (assuming same-direction currents) $F_1 = \dfrac{\mu_0 I I_1 L}{2\pi a}$ (L = length of side parallel to wire)
Far side (distance $b$): repulsive but weaker, $F_2 = \dfrac{\mu_0 I I_1 L}{2\pi b}$
Net force (towards wire, since $a<b$ so $F_1>F_2$): $F_{net} = \dfrac{\mu_0 I I_1 L}{2\pi}\left(\dfrac{1}{a}-\dfrac{1}{b}\right)$
The two sides perpendicular to wire experience forces that cancel by symmetry (equal & opposite, along the wire's length direction, net torque only if unbalanced — here they cancel in force but a full analysis for torque is possible as an extension).
**NCERT Topic:** 4.5 (Application-based, board-difficult)
**Freq:** IMP — appears as a 5-mark HOTS-style question in select years

**Q54. [5M]** A charged particle enters a uniform magnetic field at an angle $\theta$ (not 90°) to the field. Describe and derive the nature of its path (helical motion), and derive pitch of the helix.
**Answer:**
Resolve velocity: $v_\parallel = v\cos\theta$ (along B, unaffected), $v_\perp = v\sin\theta$ (perpendicular, causes circular motion).
Radius of circular component: $r = mv_\perp/qB = mv\sin\theta/qB$
Period: $T = 2\pi m/qB$ (independent of $\theta$)
Since $v_\parallel$ is constant, the particle also moves along B — combined effect is a helical path.
Pitch $p = v_\parallel T = v\cos\theta \times 2\pi m/qB = \dfrac{2\pi m v\cos\theta}{qB}$
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2 (extension — very common board-difficult question)
**Freq:** FA

### LEVEL 6 — CHALLENGE / CONCEPT MASTERY

**Q55. [5M, Olympiad-adjacent but NCERT-consistent]** Two long straight parallel wires carry equal currents $I$ in the same direction, separated by distance $2d$. Find the magnetic field at a point on the perpendicular bisector, at distance $x$ from the midpoint of the line joining the wires (point lies in the plane containing both wires and perpendicular to them).
**Answer:**
Distance of point from each wire: $r = \sqrt{d^2+x^2}$
Each wire contributes $B = \mu_0I/2\pi r$, direction tangential (perpendicular to line joining wire and point).
By symmetry, components along the line joining the wires cancel; components perpendicular (along axis of symmetry) add.
Each field makes angle $\alpha$ with the perpendicular bisector where $\sin\alpha = d/r$.
Net field $= 2B\sin\alpha = 2\times\dfrac{\mu_0I}{2\pi\sqrt{d^2+x^2}}\times\dfrac{d}{\sqrt{d^2+x^2}} = \dfrac{\mu_0Id}{\pi(d^2+x^2)}$, directed parallel to the wires' common perpendicular bisector plane (perpendicular to line joining wires, in the plane containing both wires).
**NCERT Topic:** 4.3 + 4.5 (synthesis question)
**Freq:** RBP but excellent for concept mastery

**Q56. [5M, Concept Mastery]** Explain, using the concept of atomic magnetic dipole moments, why diamagnetism is a universal property of all materials, but is often masked in paramagnetic and ferromagnetic substances.
**Answer:** Every atom has orbiting/spinning electrons, and any change in magnetic flux induces a diamagnetic response (Lenz's law at atomic level) opposing the applied field — this exists in all matter, hence diamagnetism is universal. However, in paramagnetic materials, atoms have a net unpaired electron magnetic moment that aligns with the field, producing a much stronger effect that overwhelms the weak diamagnetic response. In ferromagnetic materials, domain alignment produces an even stronger net magnetisation, completely masking diamagnetism.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.8 (deep conceptual, appears in NCERT exemplar-type questions)
**Freq:** IMP

**Q57. [5M, Concept Mastery / HOTS]** A magnetic needle free to rotate in a vertical plane oriented in the magnetic meridian shows a dip of 60° at a place where the horizontal component of Earth's field is 0.3 G. Find the total intensity of Earth's magnetic field and the vertical component.
**Answer:**
$H = B\cos\delta \Rightarrow B = H/\cos\delta = 0.3/\cos60° = 0.3/0.5 = 0.6$ G
Vertical component $V = B\sin\delta = H\tan\delta = 0.3\times\tan60° = 0.3\times1.732 = 0.52$ G
**NCERT Topic:** 5.6 (Earth's magnetism numerical)
**Freq:** FA

**Q58. [5M, Concept Mastery]** Derive an expression relating magnetic permeability $\mu_r$ and susceptibility $\chi$, and use it to classify a material as diamagnetic, paramagnetic or ferromagnetic based on the sign and magnitude of $\chi$.
**Answer:**
$B = \mu_0(H+M) = \mu_0 H(1+\chi) = \mu_0\mu_rH$, so $\mu_r = 1+\chi$.
- Diamagnetic: $\chi$ small and negative (–1 ≤ χ < 0), $\mu_r$ slightly less than 1.
- Paramagnetic: $\chi$ small and positive, $\mu_r$ slightly greater than 1.
- Ferromagnetic: $\chi$ large and positive (can be of order 1000+), $\mu_r >>1$.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.7
**Freq:** FA — this relation is a favorite for 1-mark and derivation-linked questions

**Q59. [5M, Synthesis / Challenge]** A charged particle of mass $m$, charge $q$ is projected with velocity $v$ perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field $B$ that exists only in a circular region of radius $R$ (the particle's circular radius of motion $r = mv/qB$ equals $R$). Show that the particle emerges from the field region moving perpendicular to its original direction of entry.
**Answer:** Since the particle's radius of curvature equals the radius of the field region, geometric analysis shows the chord subtended and the deflection angle work out such that the particle turns exactly 90°. (Using geometry: entry and exit points on the circular boundary, with the arc of the particle's circular path intersecting the field-boundary circle at points such that the perpendicular bisector relationships give a 90° turn when $r=R$.) This is a classic geometry-based magnetism problem testing spatial reasoning combined with circular motion in magnetic fields.
**NCERT Topic:** 4.2 (synthesis / geometry-heavy Olympiad-style, within CBSE HOTS scope)
**Freq:** RBP

**Q60. [5M, Concept Mastery]** Compare and contrast the magnetic field patterns of a bar magnet and a current-carrying solenoid. Discuss in what sense the analogy is exact and in what sense it breaks down.
**Answer:** Far from either, field lines and field magnitude formulas (axial: $B\propto 2M/d^3$; equatorial: $B\propto M/d^3$) are identical if $M = $ solenoid's magnetic moment $= NIA$. This analogy holds well for external field pattern. It breaks down internally: inside a bar magnet, the field lines run from the (conceptual) south to north pole (opposite to external convention, forming continuous loops through the magnet), while inside a solenoid, B is essentially uniform and strong ($\mu_0nI$), unlike inside a bar magnet where the concept of discrete "poles" is a mathematical convenience, not a physical separation of magnetic charge.
**NCERT Topic:** 5.2
**Freq:** IMP
