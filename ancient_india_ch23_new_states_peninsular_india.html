Main Notes
Context: The Post-Gupta Political Landscape
Following the decline of the Gupta Empire, the 6th to 8th centuries CE saw the rise of numerous regional kingdoms across the Deccan and South India, filling the political vacuum left by the earlier pan-Indian empires!
The Chalukyas of Badami
The Chalukyas ruled the western Deccan (roughly the Karnataka region), with Badami as their capital; their most famous ruler, Pulakesin II, is particularly remembered for defeating Harshavardhana and halting his southward expansion!
The Pallavas
The Pallavas ruled the Tamil region in the southeastern Deccan, with their capital at Kanchipuram, and were significant patrons of temple architecture, exemplified by the remarkable monuments at Mahabalipuram (Mamallapuram)!
The Chalukya-Pallava Rivalry
A long-standing rivalry for regional supremacy characterized relations between the Chalukyas and the Pallavas, with fortunes fluctuating between the two powers over several decades!
Feudal Characteristics of This Period
This era saw growing land grants to temples and Brahmanas, and the rise of Samantas — feudal vassals or subordinate chiefs who exercised significant local power!
Administration became considerably more decentralized compared to earlier pan-Indian empires, with local chiefs gaining increasing semi-autonomous authority!
Growth of Temple Architecture
This period witnessed major developments in temple architecture, with the Pallavas serving as early pioneers of the distinctive Dravidian architectural style, seen prominently in the monuments of Mahabalipuram!
Summary
Following Gupta decline, the 6th-8th centuries CE saw regional kingdoms rise across the Deccan and South India — the Chalukyas of Badami (western Deccan, notably Pulakesin II who defeated Harsha) and the Pallavas (Tamil region, capital Kanchipuram, pioneers of Dravidian temple architecture at Mahabalipuram) engaged in a long-standing rivalry for regional supremacy. This period was marked by growing feudal characteristics — expanding land grants to temples/Brahmanas, the rise of Samanta feudal vassals, and increasingly decentralized administration — alongside significant architectural developments that laid the foundation for the Dravidian temple style.
