Main Notes
Harshavardhana's Rule
Harshavardhana ruled from Kanauj in the 7th century CE (c. 606-647 CE), managing to unify much of North India in the wake of the political fragmentation that followed the decline of the Gupta Empire!
The Deccan Setback
Harsha's attempt to expand southward into the Deccan was checked when he was defeated by Pulakesin II, the Chalukya king — this defeat effectively established the Narmada river as the southern limit of Harsha's empire!
Religious Patronage and Assemblies
Harsha personally patronized Buddhism, though he also extended support to Brahmanical Hinduism, and organized grand religious assemblies, including a notable gathering at Kanauj and Prayaga-style religious congregations!
Hsuan Tsang's Account
The Chinese Buddhist pilgrim Hsuan Tsang visited India during Harsha's reign and wrote a detailed account of Indian society, administration, and institutions like Nalanda, serving as a key primary source for understanding this period!
Administration and Harsha as Playwright
Harsha's administration was less centralized than the earlier Mauryan or Gupta systems, relying more heavily on local feudatories and chiefs!
Harsha himself was also an accomplished playwright, credited with authoring Sanskrit dramas including Nagananda, Ratnavali, and Priyadarshika!
Nalanda under Harsha
Nalanda continued to flourish as a major center of Buddhist learning during Harsha's reign, and Hsuan Tsang himself studied there during his time in India!
Summary
Harshavardhana (r. c. 606-647 CE) ruled from Kanauj, unifying much of North India after post-Gupta fragmentation, though his southward expansion was checked by Pulakesin II of the Chalukyas, establishing the Narmada as his empire's effective southern boundary. He patronized Buddhism (while supporting Hinduism too), organized grand religious assemblies, and was himself a Sanskrit playwright (Nagananda, Ratnavali, Priyadarshika). His administration was less centralized than the Mauryas or Guptas, relying on local feudatories. The Chinese pilgrim Hsuan Tsang, who studied at the still-flourishing Nalanda during this period, left behind a detailed account that serves as a crucial primary source for understanding Harsha's era.
