Main Notes
Chandragupta Maurya
Chandragupta Maurya founded the Mauryan Empire around 322 BCE, overthrowing the last ruler of the Nanda dynasty, with crucial guidance from his mentor Chanakya (also known as Kautilya), the author of the Arthashastra, a treatise on statecraft!
Later in life, Chandragupta abdicated the throne and became a Jain ascetic!
Bindusara
Bindusara, Chandragupta's son, further expanded the Mauryan Empire southward into the Deccan region!
Ashoka
Ashoka is considered the greatest Mauryan ruler; the Kalinga War (261 BCE) marked a pivotal turning point in his reign — witnessing the massive casualties of this brutal conflict led him to deep remorse, prompting his adoption of Buddhism and the policy of Dhamma, an ethical framework for governance!
Ashoka's edicts were inscribed on pillars and rocks throughout his empire, written primarily in the Brahmi script, though also in Kharoshthi, Greek, and Aramaic scripts in the northwestern regions — these were deciphered by James Prinsep in 1837!
Dhamma was not a religion in itself but an ethical code emphasizing non-violence, tolerance, respect for elders, and general welfare; Ashoka also sent Buddhist missionaries abroad, notably to Sri Lanka!
Mauryan Administration
The Mauryan administrative system was highly centralized, with the empire divided into provinces governed by appointees, often royal princes!
A sophisticated espionage/spy network helped the central authority maintain control; officials such as Rajukas (responsible for justice) and Mahamatras (dhamma officers overseeing ethical governance) played key administrative roles!
Key Sources
The Arthashastra by Kautilya provides detailed insight into Mauryan statecraft and administration; the Indica, written by the Greek ambassador Megasthenes, offers a foreign observer's account of Mauryan society and governance; and the Ashokan edicts themselves serve as invaluable primary inscriptional sources!
Summary
Chandragupta Maurya founded the Mauryan Empire (c. 322 BCE) with Chanakya's guidance, later becoming a Jain ascetic; his son Bindusara expanded the empire into the Deccan. Ashoka, the greatest Mauryan ruler, adopted Buddhism and the ethical policy of Dhamma after the transformative Kalinga War (261 BCE), inscribing edicts across the empire in multiple scripts (Brahmi, Kharoshthi, Greek, Aramaic), later deciphered by James Prinsep (1837). Mauryan administration was highly centralized, with provincial governors, an extensive spy network, and officials like Rajukas and Mahamatras. Key sources for this period include Kautilya's Arthashastra, Megasthenes's Indica, and the Ashokan edicts themselves.
