Main Notes
The Persian (Iranian) Invasion
Around 516 BCE, the Achaemenid Persian king Darius I annexed parts of northwestern India, particularly the Indus region, incorporating it as a satrapy (province) of the Persian Empire!
This Persian presence introduced administrative practices and the Aramaic script into northwestern India, and facilitated increased trade and cultural contact between India and Persia!
Alexander's Invasion
In 326 BCE, Alexander the Great, the Macedonian king, crossed into India through the northwestern mountain passes!
He fought the famous Battle of Hydaspes against King Porus, the ruler of the Punjab region, on the banks of the river Hydaspes (identified with the modern Jhelum)!
Although Alexander won the battle, he was reportedly so impressed by Porus's courage and bravery in battle that he restored Porus's kingdom to him rather than annexing it directly!
Alexander's Retreat
Alexander's advance into India was halted not by defeat, but by a mutiny among his own soldiers, who were exhausted, homesick, and apprehensive about facing the larger armies believed to await them further east, particularly those of the powerful Magadhan (Nanda) empire!
Alexander turned back and died shortly afterward, in 323 BCE, in Babylon!
Impact of These Invasions
These invasions opened up land routes connecting India, Persia, and Greece, facilitating cultural and commercial exchange!
They influenced the later development of Indo-Greek art, which would eventually contribute to the roots of the Gandhara school of art!
Persian administrative practices, particularly the satrapy model of provincial governance, are believed to have influenced the later provincial administrative system developed under the Mauryan Empire!
Greek historical accounts, such as those by the historian Arrian, serve as a valuable source of information about this period of Indian history!
Summary
Persian King Darius I annexed parts of northwestern India as a satrapy around 516 BCE, introducing administrative practices and the Aramaic script. In 326 BCE, Alexander the Great invaded via the northwestern passes, defeating King Porus at the Battle of Hydaspes (Jhelum river) but restoring his kingdom out of respect for his bravery. Alexander's advance was halted by a mutiny among his own exhausted soldiers (not by Indian defeat), and he died in 323 BCE shortly after retreating. These invasions opened land trade routes between India, Persia, and Greece, influenced later Indo-Greek/Gandhara art, shaped Mauryan provincial administration through the satrapy model, and left behind valuable Greek historical accounts (like Arrian's) documenting the period.
