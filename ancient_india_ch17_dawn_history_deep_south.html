Main Notes
The Sangam Age
The Sangam Age refers to the early historic period of the Tamil region, roughly spanning 300 BCE to 300 CE, named after "Sangam" — assemblies or academies of Tamil poets that flourished during this era!
Sangam Literature
This period produced a rich body of Tamil poetry, broadly categorized into Akam (poems on love and personal life) and Puram (poems on war and heroism)!
Key texts include the Tolkappiyam (a foundational work on Tamil grammar), and anthologies such as the Ettuthogai and Pathupattu!
The Three Early Kingdoms
The Cheras ruled in the west (roughly the present Kerala region), with their capital at Vanji, and were prominent in the spice trade!
The Cholas ruled the eastern/central Tamil region, initially from Uraiyur and later from Kaveripattinam!
The Pandyas ruled in the south, with their capital at Madurai, and were known for their pearl fisheries!
Trade with Rome
Major ports such as Muziris and Arikamedu facilitated extensive Indo-Roman trade, with the Tamil region exporting pepper, pearls, and textiles!
The extensive discovery of Roman gold coins across the Tamil region provides strong archaeological evidence of this flourishing trade relationship!
Megalithic Culture and Society
The megalithic culture, characterized by large stone monuments marking burial sites, is associated with the Iron Age in South India and predates or overlaps with the early Sangam period!
Society during this period was organized along chieftain-based, tribal-clan lines, with a strong warrior ethos celebrated extensively in Puram poetry — distinct from the rigid varna-based social structure prevalent in North India at this stage!
Summary
The Sangam Age (c. 300 BCE-300 CE) marks the dawn of recorded history in Tamil South India, named after the poetic academies (Sangams) that produced rich literature (Akam/love poetry, Puram/war poetry; key texts: Tolkappiyam, Ettuthogai, Pathupattu). Three early kingdoms — Chera (west, spice trade), Chola (east/central), and Pandya (south, pearl fisheries) — dominated the region, with major ports like Muziris and Arikamedu facilitating extensive Indo-Roman trade (evidenced by widespread Roman gold coin finds). The megalithic Iron Age culture and a chieftain-based, warrior-ethos society distinguished this region from North India's more rigid varna-based social structure at the time.
