Main Notes
Principles of Foreign Policy
India's foreign policy has historically been guided by the Panchsheel principles (Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence), agreed with China in 1954: mutual respect for territorial integrity and sovereignty, mutual non-aggression, non-interference in internal affairs, equality and mutual benefit, and peaceful coexistence — though these were later undermined by China in the 1962 war!
Other core principles include anti-colonialism and anti-imperialism, the historic policy of Non-Alignment (avoiding formal alliance with either superpower bloc during the Cold War), promotion of South-South cooperation with other developing nations, and pursuit of international harmony through the United Nations and multilateral diplomacy!
Gujral Doctrine
Named after I.K. Gujral, who served as External Affairs Minister and later Prime Minister, this doctrine holds that India should extend asymmetric concessions to its smaller neighbors, without insisting on strict reciprocity!
The aim was to build trust and goodwill with India's immediate neighborhood — particularly Bangladesh, Nepal, Bhutan, and Sri Lanka — thereby strengthening regional stability and India's own strategic position!
Nuclear Doctrine of India
India follows a "No First Use" (NFU) nuclear policy, committing not to use nuclear weapons first against any adversary!
The doctrine is built around the concept of credible minimum deterrence, but promises massive retaliation if India is ever attacked with nuclear weapons!
India's nuclear arsenal remains under firm civilian political control, rather than being controlled by the military establishment!
Connect Central Asia Policy of India
Launched in 2012, this policy aims to strengthen India's political, economic, and security ties with the Central Asian republics — Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, Kyrgyzstan, and Tajikistan!
Act East Policy of India
The Act East Policy is an upgraded and more proactive version of India's earlier "Look East Policy," which was launched in 1991 primarily with economic objectives!
Renamed and reoriented in 2014, the Act East Policy places greater emphasis on strategic and security engagement, in addition to economic and cultural ties, with ASEAN member states and other East Asian nations!
Summary
India's foreign policy is historically anchored in the Panchsheel principles (1954, agreed with China), anti-colonialism, Non-Alignment, South-South cooperation, and multilateral engagement through the UN. The Gujral Doctrine advocates asymmetric, non-reciprocal concessions to smaller neighbors to build regional trust. India's Nuclear Doctrine follows a No First Use policy with credible minimum deterrence and civilian control over the arsenal. The Connect Central Asia Policy (2012) strengthens ties with Central Asian republics, while the Act East Policy (2014) — an upgrade of the 1991 Look East Policy — deepens economic, strategic, and cultural engagement with ASEAN and East Asian nations.
