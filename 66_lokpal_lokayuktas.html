Main Notes
Global Scenario
The concept of an anti-corruption/grievance-redress ombudsman originated from the Scandinavian "Ombudsman" institution, first established in Sweden in 1809!
Several countries subsequently adopted similar institutions — for example, New Zealand and the UK (Parliamentary Commissioner for Administration) — to provide citizens a channel for redress against maladministration and corruption!
Position in India
The idea was first formally proposed by the Administrative Reforms Commission in 1966, which recommended a "Lokpal" at the Union level and "Lokayukta" at the state level!
Several Lokpal Bills were introduced in Parliament starting from 1968, but all repeatedly lapsed due to dissolution of the Lok Sabha or lack of political consensus!
The Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act was finally enacted only in 2013, following widespread public pressure generated by the Anna Hazare-led anti-corruption movement in 2011!
Lokpal
Lokpal is a statutory body, not a constitutional one!
Composed of a Chairperson and a maximum of 8 members; at least 50% of the members must be judicial members, and at least 50% must be from SC/ST/OBC/women/minority backgrounds!
Appointed by the President on the recommendation of a Selection Committee comprising the PM, Speaker of Lok Sabha, Leader of Opposition in Lok Sabha, Chief Justice of India (or a Supreme Court judge nominated by him), and an eminent jurist!
Jurisdiction covers the Prime Minister (subject to certain safeguards/exclusions relating to matters of international relations, security, public order, atomic energy, and space), Union Ministers, Members of Parliament, and Group A/B/C/D employees of the Central Government, as well as officials of government-financed societies, trusts, and boards, and any society/trust receiving foreign contributions above a prescribed limit!
Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013
Mandates every state to establish a Lokayukta within one year of the Act's commencement (though several states delayed compliance considerably)!
Provides for an Inquiry Wing and a Prosecution Wing to support Lokpal's investigative and prosecutorial functions!
Empowers attachment of assets, proceeds, or benefits acquired through corrupt means during the course of investigation!
Prescribes timelines — inquiry to be completed within 60 days, and investigation within 6 months, extendable in specific circumstances!
Lokayuktas
Lokayuktas are state-level anti-corruption ombudsman institutions!
Maharashtra was the first state to establish a Lokayukta, in 1971, well before the 2013 central Act was even enacted!
Unlike the uniform structure of the Lokpal (governed by a single central law), the composition, appointment process, and functions of Lokayuktas VARY significantly from state to state, as they are governed by individual state legislation!
Lokayuktas generally provide a mechanism for grievance redress against corruption by state government officials and ministers!
Summary
The ombudsman concept originated in Scandinavia (Sweden, 1809) and spread globally. In India, first proposed by the ARC in 1966 (Lokpal at Centre, Lokayukta at states), but repeated bills lapsed until the Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013 was finally enacted after the 2011 Anna Hazare movement. Lokpal is a statutory (not constitutional) body — Chairperson + up to 8 members (50% judicial, 50% from SC/ST/OBC/women/minorities), appointed by the President via a Selection Committee, with jurisdiction over the PM (with safeguards), Ministers, MPs, central govt employees, and government-financed/foreign-funded entities. The 2013 Act mandated states to establish Lokayuktas within a year, created Inquiry/Prosecution Wings, enabled asset attachment, and set inquiry/investigation timelines. Lokayuktas, however, predate the 2013 Act (Maharashtra, 1971) and vary widely in structure across states, unlike the uniform Lokpal framework.
