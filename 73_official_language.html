Main Notes
Language of the Union
Art 343 declares Hindi in the Devanagari script as the official language of the Union, with the international form of Indian numerals to be used for official purposes (Parliament can change this by law)!
English was to continue being used for official purposes for a period of 15 years from the Constitution's commencement (1950-1965)!
The Official Languages Act, 1963 extended the use of English INDEFINITELY alongside Hindi, essentially making India bilingual for official Union purposes!
Regional Language
Art 345 empowers a State Legislature to adopt Hindi, or any other language in use within the state, as the official language(s) of that state!
Art 346 provides that the language for communication between the Union and a State, or between one State and another, shall be Hindi or English, unless two or more states agree to use Hindi between themselves!
Art 347 allows the President to direct that a language spoken by a substantial proportion of a state's population be officially recognized for a specified purpose within that state!
Languages of the Judiciary & Texts of Laws
Art 348 mandates that all proceedings in the Supreme Court and every High Court, along with the authoritative texts of all Bills, Acts, Ordinances, Orders, Rules, Regulations, and Bye-laws, be in ENGLISH, unless Parliament by law provides otherwise!
A Governor may, with the President's prior consent, authorize the use of Hindi or another official state language in High Court proceedings — but the authoritative text of any judgment, decree, or order remains in English!
Special Directives
Art 350 guarantees every person the right to submit a representation for redress of grievances in any language used in the Union or in the State, as the case may be!
Art 350A directs states/local authorities to provide adequate mother-tongue instruction facilities at the primary stage for children of linguistic minority groups!
Art 350B provides for a Special Officer for Linguistic Minorities to investigate matters relating to linguistic minority safeguards!
Art 351 places a duty on the Union to promote the spread of Hindi and develop it as a medium of expression for India's composite culture, drawing vocabulary primarily from Sanskrit and secondarily from other Indian languages!
Committee of Parliament on Official Language
Constituted in 1976 under Section 4 of the Official Languages Act, 1963!
Comprises 30 members — 20 from the Lok Sabha and 10 from the Rajya Sabha!
Its task is to review the progress made in the use of Hindi for official purposes of the Union, and submit a report to the President, who causes it to be laid before both Houses of Parliament and forwarded to all state governments!
Classical Language Status
The Ministry of Culture grants "Classical Language" status based on criteria such as high antiquity of early texts/recorded history (spanning 1500-2000 years), a body of ancient literature/texts considered a valuable heritage, an original literary tradition not borrowed from another speech community, and sufficient distinctiveness from its modern forms!
Tamil was the first language to be granted classical status, in 2004, followed by Sanskrit (2005), Kannada and Telugu (2008), Malayalam (2013), Odia (2014), and more recently Marathi, Pali, Prakrit, Assamese, and Bengali (2024)!
Benefits of classical status include international awards for scholars of the language, establishment of centers of excellence for classical language studies, and dedicated university chairs!
Summary
Hindi (Devanagari script) is the official language of the Union (Art 343), with English's use extended indefinitely by the Official Languages Act 1963. States can adopt their own official languages (Art 345); inter-state/Union-state communication uses Hindi or English (Art 346); Art 347 lets the President recognize a substantial minority language within a state. Judicial proceedings and authoritative legal texts remain in English by default (Art 348), even where Hindi/local languages are permitted for proceedings. Special directives protect linguistic minorities — right to petition in any language (Art 350), mother-tongue primary education (Art 350A), a dedicated Linguistic Minorities Officer (Art 350B), and a duty to develop Hindi (Art 351). A 30-member Parliamentary Committee on Official Language (since 1976) reviews Hindi's official usage progress, reporting to the President. The Ministry of Culture grants Classical Language status based on antiquity/heritage/originality criteria — Tamil was first (2004), followed by Sanskrit, Kannada, Telugu, Malayalam, Odia, and more recently Marathi, Pali, Prakrit, Assamese, and Bengali (2024).
