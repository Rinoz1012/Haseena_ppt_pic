Main Notes
The Partition of Bengal (1905)
Lord Curzon's controversial decision to administratively divide Bengal was widely perceived by Indians as a deliberate "divide-and-rule" strategy aimed at weakening Bengali political unity, and it sparked massive, widespread protest across the region!
The Swadeshi Movement
Emerging directly from this anti-partition protest, the Swadeshi Movement promoted the active boycott of British manufactured goods alongside vigorous promotion of indigenous (Swadeshi) products and industries, representing a significant new form of economic nationalism!
Rise of Assertive Nationalism
This period saw the rise of more assertive nationalist leadership, exemplified by the "Lal-Bal-Pal" trio — Lala Lajpat Rai, Bal Gangadhar Tilak, and Bipin Chandra Pal — who advocated more assertive, mass-oriented political methods compared to the earlier Moderate approach!
The Congress Split (1907)
This growing ideological divide culminated in a formal split within the Indian National Congress at its 1907 Surat session, dividing the movement between its more cautious Moderate faction and its more assertive Extremist faction!
Formation of the Muslim League (1906)
The All-India Muslim League was founded in 1906 specifically to represent and advance Muslim political interests as a distinct political voice within India's evolving political landscape!
World War I Context
India's significant participation in the British war effort during World War I, combined with unmet expectations of substantial political reward or reform in return, contributed significantly to growing nationalist assertiveness and disillusionment by 1918!
Summary
The 1905-1918 period marked a decisive shift toward more assertive Indian nationalism, triggered by Lord Curzon's controversial Partition of Bengal (1905), which sparked the Swadeshi Movement's boycott of British goods and promotion of indigenous industry. This period saw the rise of more assertive leadership through the "Lal-Bal-Pal" trio (Lajpat Rai, Tilak, Pal), culminating in the formal Congress split between Moderates and Extremists at the 1907 Surat session. The Muslim League was also founded in 1906 to represent distinct Muslim political interests. World War I's demands on India, combined with unmet expectations of political reward for wartime support, further fueled growing nationalist assertiveness by 1918, setting the stage for the mass movements that would follow.
