Main Notes
Meaning
Voting behaviour refers to the study of how and why people vote the way they do, analyzing patterns across factors such as caste, class, religion, region, gender, and political ideology!
Significance
Understanding voting behaviour helps analyze the nature and depth of democratic participation in a society!
It explains electoral outcomes and shifts in political power, and informs policy interventions aimed at improving voter education and turnout!
It forms the basis of psephology — the systematic study and forecasting of elections — which has become increasingly significant in Indian political analysis!
Determinants
Caste has historically been one of the strongest determinants of voting behaviour in India, and while its influence persists, it is increasingly moderated by other factors!
Class/economic status, religion, and gender also shape voting patterns, though the traditional gender voting gap has narrowed considerably over recent decades!
Party loyalty and ideological alignment, along with the appeal of the local candidate, continue to influence voter choice!
Regional and linguistic identity remains a powerful determinant, especially in states with strong regional parties!
Leadership charisma and personality-driven campaigning have become increasingly significant in shaping voter preference in recent elections!
Anti-incumbency sentiment, and the perceived performance of the government in delivering welfare schemes and governance outcomes, have emerged as major determinants of voting behaviour in contemporary Indian elections!
Summary
Voting behaviour studies how and why people vote as they do, and is significant for understanding democratic participation, explaining electoral outcomes, and informing psephological analysis. Determinants traditionally include caste, class, religion, and gender, alongside party loyalty, candidate appeal, and regional/linguistic identity. In recent decades, leadership charisma, anti-incumbency sentiment, and governance/welfare-delivery performance have become increasingly influential factors, moderating the traditional dominance of caste and religion-based voting.
