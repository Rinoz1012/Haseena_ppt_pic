Main Notes
Chandragupta I
Chandragupta I founded the Gupta Empire around 320 CE, strengthening his position through a matrimonial alliance with Kumaradevi, a princess of the Lichchhavi clan!
His accession is generally taken to mark the beginning of the Gupta Era (320 CE)!
Samudragupta
Samudragupta, often referred to by historians as the "Napoleon of India" due to his extensive military conquests, significantly expanded the Gupta Empire's territory!
His conquests are recorded in the Prayaga Prashasti (the Allahabad Pillar Inscription), composed by his court poet Harishena, which serves as a crucial primary source for this period!
Chandragupta II (Vikramaditya)
Samudragupta's son, Chandragupta II, presided over what is often considered the peak of the Gupta "Golden Age," defeating the Shakas (Western Kshatrapas)!
His court reportedly hosted the "Navaratnas" (Nine Gems), a group of celebrated scholars and poets including the renowned Kalidasa!
The Chinese Buddhist pilgrim Fa-Hien visited India during Chandragupta II's reign, leaving behind valuable observations of Gupta society!
Administration
Gupta administration was less centralized than the earlier Mauryan system, allowing for greater local autonomy!
Officials such as Kumaramatyas (high officials) and Vishayapatis (district-level officers) administered various levels of the Gupta administrative structure!
Decline of the Gupta Empire
The Gupta Empire's decline is attributed to several factors: invasions by the Hunas (a Central Asian tribe) that weakened the empire militarily, growing feudalization as land grants created increasingly semi-independent local chiefs, the weakness of later Gupta successors, and a resulting loss of central control over the empire's provinces!
Summary
Chandragupta I founded the Gupta Empire (c. 320 CE) through matrimonial alliance with the Lichchhavis, starting the Gupta Era. Samudragupta ("Napoleon of India") vastly expanded the empire, documented in the Allahabad Pillar Inscription (Harishena). Chandragupta II (Vikramaditya) presided over the Golden Age peak, defeating the Shakas, hosting the Navaratnas (including Kalidasa), and being visited by the Chinese pilgrim Fa-Hien. Gupta administration was less centralized than the Mauryan model, relying on officials like Kumaramatyas and Vishayapatis. The empire's decline stemmed from Huna invasions, growing feudalization through land grants, weak later successors, and loss of central control over the provinces.
