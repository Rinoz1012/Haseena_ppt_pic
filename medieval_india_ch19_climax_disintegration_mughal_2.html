Main Notes
The Rise of the Marathas; Early Career of Shivaji
The Marathas emerged as a formidable new regional power under the leadership of Shivaji, who built an effective state and military organization in western India, significantly challenging established regional and Mughal authority!
Treaty of Purandar and Shivaji's Visit to Agra
Shivaji's relationship with the Mughals included the Treaty of Purandar and a subsequent, ultimately tense, visit to the Mughal court at Agra, episodes that shaped the trajectory of Maratha-Mughal relations!
Final Breach with Shivaji — Administration and Achievements
Shivaji eventually broke decisively from Mughal authority, going on to establish a significant and well-organized independent administration, considered among his most notable achievements!
Aurangzeb and the Deccani States (1658-1707)
Aurangzeb's reign involved a prolonged, multi-decade conflict with the Deccan states, unfolding across three broad phases — the First Phase (1658-68), the Second Phase (1668-84), and the Third Phase (1684-87) — representing an enormous and sustained commitment of Mughal military and financial resources!
Aurangzeb, the Marathas and the Deccan — the Last Phase (1687-1707)
The final phase of this conflict (1687-1707) proved to be the most difficult and draining for the Mughals, as Maratha resistance persisted despite continued Mughal military campaigns!
Decline of the Mughal Empire — Responsibility of Aurangzeb
Historians continue to debate the extent to which Aurangzeb's policies and prolonged military campaigns were personally responsible for the Mughal Empire's subsequent decline, versus broader structural and economic factors at play!
Summary
The later phase of Mughal history was dominated by the rise of the Marathas under Shivaji, whose early career (including the Treaty of Purandar and his tense Agra visit) culminated in a final decisive breach with Mughal authority and the establishment of an effective independent administration. Aurangzeb's reign became consumed by a prolonged, three-phase conflict with the Deccani states (1658-87), followed by an even more grueling final phase against the Marathas specifically (1687-1707) that drained Mughal resources without achieving decisive victory. This sustained conflict, combined with other structural pressures, contributed significantly to the empire's decline — though historians continue to debate how much personal responsibility rests with Aurangzeb himself versus broader underlying factors.
