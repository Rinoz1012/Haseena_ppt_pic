Main Notes
Discovery and Overview
The Harappan Civilization was a Bronze Age civilization, with its mature urban phase dated to around 2500-1900 BCE, located along the Indus river and its tributaries!
Harappa was discovered in 1921 by Dayaram Sahni, and Mohenjodaro in 1922 by R.D. Banerji, revealing a sophisticated urban civilization!
Major Sites
Harappa (Punjab, Pakistan) is known for its granaries and nearby cemetery; Mohenjodaro (Sindh, Pakistan), the largest site, features the Great Bath, a public structure believed to serve ritual bathing purposes!
Dholavira (Gujarat) shows sophisticated water reservoir systems; Lothal (Gujarat) had a dockyard indicating maritime trade; Kalibangan (Rajasthan) revealed fire altars and evidence of a ploughed field; and Rakhigarhi (Haryana) is currently the largest known Harappan site within India!
Town Planning, Economy, Society
Harappan cities followed a grid-pattern layout, divided into a citadel (upper town) and lower town, with an advanced urban drainage system and standardized bricks (1:2:4 ratio) used across sites!
The economy relied on agriculture (wheat, barley, and cotton — Harappans were the first known cultivators of cotton) and trade with Mesopotamia, evidenced by Harappan seals discovered there; weights and measures were standardized, though no coinage existed, suggesting a barter-based trade system!
Religious practices are inferred from seals depicting a "Pashupati" figure (a proto-Shiva deity) and various animals, along with terracotta Mother Goddess figurines; however, no clear evidence of temple structures has been found, and the Indus script remains undeciphered to this day!
Political Organization
Unlike contemporary Bronze Age civilizations such as Egypt and Mesopotamia, no clear evidence of kingship, monarchy, or royal palaces has been found at Harappan sites!
Scholars continue to debate the nature of Harappan political organization — some suggest governance by a merchant oligarchy or priestly class, but this remains an unresolved and actively debated question!
Decline
Several theories have been proposed for the civilization's decline, including the now largely discredited "Aryan invasion" theory, climate change and drying up of rivers (possibly the Saraswati), recurring floods, tectonic shifts, and deforestation!
Summary
The Harappan Civilization (c. 2500-1900 BCE), discovered at Harappa (1921) and Mohenjodaro (1922), featured grid-planned cities with advanced drainage and standardized bricks/weights, across sites including Dholavira, Lothal, Kalibangan, and Rakhigarhi. Its economy relied on agriculture (including the world's earliest cotton cultivation) and Mesopotamian trade; religious life is inferred from seals and figurines, but the script remains undeciphered and no temples have been confirmed. Uniquely among Bronze Age civilizations, no clear evidence of kingship or monarchy has been found, leaving its political organization a subject of ongoing debate. Decline theories include climate change, river drying, floods, and deforestation, with the earlier Aryan invasion theory now largely discredited.
