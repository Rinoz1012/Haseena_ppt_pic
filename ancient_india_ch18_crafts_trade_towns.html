Main Notes
Growth of Towns
The post-Mauryan age (roughly 200 BCE-300 CE) witnessed significant growth of towns and cities across India, driven primarily by the expansion of trade and increasing specialization in various crafts!
Guilds (Shrenis)
Merchants and artisans organized themselves into guilds (Shrenis), which regulated prices, product quality, and apprenticeship practices for various trades and crafts!
In some cases, these guilds functioned almost like early banking institutions, accepting deposits and extending loans, playing a significant role in the period's economic life!
Trade Routes
Overland trade flowed through the Silk Route, connecting India with Central Asia, China, and ultimately Rome!
Maritime trade routes across the Indian Ocean connected India with the Red Sea, Arabia, and Southeast Asia, facilitating extensive commercial exchange!
Indo-Roman Trade
India exported spices, textiles, precious gems, and ivory to Rome, while Rome paid largely in gold and silver coins — this created a significant trade imbalance that Roman writers, such as Pliny, explicitly noted and lamented as a drain of Roman wealth to India!
Coinage and Craft Specialization
Different regions and dynasties issued distinctive coinage during this period — the Kushans issued gold coins in the northwest, the Satavahanas issued coins largely of lead and copper in the Deccan, and Roman gold coins are found extensively across South India as a result of trade!
Craft specialization flourished during this period across textiles (cotton and silk), metalworking (iron and bronze), bead-making, and ivory carving, driven by growing urban demand and expanding export markets!
Summary
The post-Mauryan age (c. 200 BCE-300 CE) saw significant urban growth driven by expanding trade and craft specialization. Merchant/artisan guilds (Shrenis) regulated trade practices and sometimes functioned as early banking institutions. Overland trade flowed via the Silk Route (India-Central Asia-China-Rome) while maritime trade connected India with the Red Sea, Arabia, and Southeast Asia. Indo-Roman trade saw India export spices, textiles, gems, and ivory in exchange for gold/silver — a trade imbalance explicitly lamented by Roman writers like Pliny. Distinctive regional coinage (Kushan gold, Satavahana lead/copper, plus extensive Roman gold coin finds) and flourishing craft specialization (textiles, metalworking, bead-making, ivory carving) characterized this economically vibrant period.
