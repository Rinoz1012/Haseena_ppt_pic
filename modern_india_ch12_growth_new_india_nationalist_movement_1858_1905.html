Main Notes
Early Political Associations
Prior to the formation of the Indian National Congress, various regional political associations emerged across India, representing the growing political consciousness and interests of educated Indians in different provinces!
Founding of the Indian National Congress
The Indian National Congress (INC) was founded in 1885, initially conceived and functioning primarily as a moderate forum for dialogue and petition with the British government, rather than as a mass political movement!
Moderate Phase Leadership and Methods
Leaders during this early "Moderate" phase — including figures like Dadabhai Naoroji and Gopal Krishna Gokhale — advocated constitutional methods and gradual reform, generally expressing loyalty to the British Crown while seeking greater Indian participation in governance and administration!
These early nationalist leaders relied primarily on methods such as formal petitions, passed resolutions, constitutional agitation, and appeals to the British sense of justice and fair play, rather than more confrontational or mass-mobilization approaches!
Growing Disillusionment
Over time, the limited responsiveness of the British government to these moderate demands and petitions gradually fueled growing disillusionment among many Indians, contributing to the emergence of more assertive nationalist sentiment!
Setting the Stage
This period (1858-1905) laid crucial organizational and ideological groundwork that would enable the more assertive and mass-oriented nationalist movement to emerge after 1905!
Summary
The nationalist movement's early phase (1858-1905) saw the emergence of various regional political associations before the founding of the Indian National Congress in 1885, which initially functioned as a moderate forum for dialogue with British authorities rather than a mass movement. Moderate leaders like Dadabhai Naoroji and Gopal Krishna Gokhale relied on constitutional methods — petitions, resolutions, and appeals to British fairness — while maintaining general loyalty to the Crown. However, the limited responsiveness of British authorities to these moderate approaches gradually generated growing disillusionment, laying essential organizational and ideological groundwork for the more assertive nationalist movement that would emerge after 1905.
