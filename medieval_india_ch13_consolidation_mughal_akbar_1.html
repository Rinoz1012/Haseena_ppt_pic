Main Notes
Early Phase — Contest with the Nobility (1556-67)
In his early reign, Akbar had to assert his personal authority against powerful nobles who had considerable influence during his minority, gradually establishing firm central control!
Early Expansion of the Empire (1560-76)
Akbar undertook significant military campaigns during this period, substantially expanding Mughal territorial control across North India!
Administration; Mansabdari System and the Army
Akbar developed a highly centralized administrative system supported by a well-organized bureaucracy, considered one of the most effective administrative frameworks of the medieval period!
He introduced the Mansabdari system, a rank-based structure that integrated military command with administrative responsibilities, forming the backbone of Mughal governance and military organization!
Organization of Government
This period saw the development of a structured administrative hierarchy extending across the entire empire, enabling more effective governance of Mughal territories!
Rebellions and Further Expansion of the Mughal Empire
Despite periodic rebellions that Akbar had to suppress, Mughal territorial expansion continued steadily throughout his reign!
Towards Integration — State, Religion and Social Reforms
Akbar pursued significant policies aimed at integrating India's diverse population, including the abolition of the jizya tax on non-Muslims, promotion of religious tolerance, and his own syncretic religious philosophy known as Din-i-Ilahi!
Summary
Akbar's reign marked the true consolidation of the Mughal Empire — beginning with asserting authority over powerful nobles, followed by significant territorial expansion (1560-76). His administrative innovations, particularly the Mansabdari system integrating military and administrative roles, created an effective centralized governance structure. Despite periodic rebellions, Akbar continued expanding the empire while pursuing integrative policies — abolishing the jizya tax, promoting religious tolerance, and introducing his syncretic Din-i-Ilahi philosophy — aimed at unifying his diverse subjects under Mughal rule.
