Main Notes
Government of India Act, 1858
This landmark Act formally ended East India Company rule in India, transferring governing power directly to the British Crown — a direct consequence of the 1857 revolt exposing the risks of Company rule!
Secretary of State for India and the Viceroy
A new position was created within the British Cabinet specifically responsible for Indian affairs, assisted by an advisory Council of India, replacing the earlier Company-based administrative structure!
The title of Viceroy was given to the Governor-General as the direct representative of the British Crown in India, symbolizing the transition to direct royal authority over Indian governance!
Indian Councils Acts
A series of Indian Councils Acts (1861, 1892, and 1909) gradually introduced limited Indian representation within legislative councils, though these bodies continued to operate with significantly restricted powers and a narrow, limited franchise!
Policy Shift Toward Caution
Following the shock of the 1857 revolt, British administrators adopted a markedly more cautious and conservative approach to social reform, generally avoiding actions that might be perceived as provocative to Indian religious or social sentiments!
Army Reorganization
The British Indian Army was significantly restructured after 1857 specifically to reduce the risk of future rebellion — altering recruitment patterns to favor certain groups over others, and substantially increasing the proportion of British troops relative to Indian sepoys!
Summary
Following the 1857 revolt, the Government of India Act (1858) formally ended East India Company rule, transferring governance directly to the British Crown, with a new Secretary of State for India created in the British Cabinet and the Governor-General retitled Viceroy. Subsequent Indian Councils Acts (1861, 1892, 1909) gradually introduced limited Indian representation in legislative councils, though with restricted powers and franchise. This period also saw a marked shift toward administrative caution regarding social reform, and significant army reorganization aimed at reducing the risk of future rebellion through altered recruitment patterns and increased British troop ratios.
