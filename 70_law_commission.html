Main Notes
Historical Background
The Law Commission concept traces back to pre-Independence India — the first Law Commission was constituted in 1834 under Lord Macaulay, following the Charter Act of 1833, and it was responsible for drafting the Indian Penal Code!
Subsequent colonial-era Law Commissions were constituted in 1853, 1861, and 1879!
Post-Independence, the first Law Commission of Independent India was constituted in 1955!
Commissions Constituted So Far
The Law Commission is periodically reconstituted, usually for a term of about 3 years!
It is a non-permanent, non-statutory body — it is set up through an executive notification of the Government, and reconstituted afresh (often with some delay/gap) after the term of each preceding Commission expires!
Composition
Headed by a Chairman, usually a retired judge of the Supreme Court or a High Court, or an eminent jurist!
Comprises full-time members, part-time members, and ex-officio members — typically the Secretary of the Legislative Department and the Secretary of the Department of Legal Affairs!
Functions
Reviews and examines existing laws in light of changing needs, and recommends the repeal of obsolete or unnecessary legislation!
Suggests enactment of new legislation as required by evolving socio-economic conditions!
Examines laws from the perspective of gender equality and elimination of unnecessary litigation/delays!
Undertakes research on general legal/juridical principles, and responds to specific referrals made by the government on particular legal issues!
Working & Role
Functions as an advisory body attached to the Ministry of Law and Justice, submitting reports to the government on subjects referred to it or taken up on its own initiative!
Its reports and recommendations are not binding on the government!
Despite this, the Law Commission has contributed significantly to numerous law reforms in India over the decades — including reforms in criminal law, family law, and evidence law — though actual implementation depends entirely on the government's political will, given its purely advisory, non-statutory character!
Summary
The Law Commission of India traces its roots to the 1834 Macaulay Commission (which drafted the IPC), with the first post-Independence Commission constituted in 1955. It is a non-permanent, non-statutory body, reconstituted every ~3 years via executive notification, headed by a Chairman (usually a retired judge/eminent jurist) with full-time, part-time, and ex-officio members. Functions include reviewing existing laws, recommending repeal of obsolete legislation, suggesting new laws, examining gender equality and litigation-delay issues, and responding to government referrals. It works as an advisory body under the Ministry of Law and Justice — reports are not binding, but the Commission has significantly influenced law reform in India over time, subject entirely to government's willingness to act on its recommendations.
