Main Notes
Constitutional Provisions
97th Constitutional Amendment Act, 2011 made three key changes: added "or cooperative societies" to Art 19(1)(c), making the right to form cooperative societies a fundamental right; inserted Part IX-B "The Co-operative Societies" (Art 243ZH to 243ZT); and added Art 43B to the DPSP, directing the State to promote voluntary formation, autonomous functioning, democratic member-control, and professional management of cooperative societies!
Part IX-B laid down detailed provisions — a maximum of 21 directors on a cooperative society's board, a fixed 5-year term for board members, mandatory elections before expiry of term (overseen by a State Cooperative Election Authority), compulsory audit of accounts, filing of returns, and members' right to information!
Reason for 97th Amendment
Cooperative societies in India had long suffered from excessive government interference, financial mismanagement, and a lack of professional management!
Elections to cooperative society boards were frequently delayed, and societies were often superseded by state governments without adequate justification!
The amendment aimed to bring cooperative societies under a constitutional protection framework similar to what the 73rd and 74th Amendments provided for panchayats and municipalities, ensuring their autonomy, democratic functioning, and accountability!
Constitutional Validity of 97th Amendment
The amendment was challenged before the Gujarat High Court in the Rajendra Shah case!
The core argument was that "cooperative societies" falls under Entry 32 of the STATE List — an exclusive state subject — and Part IX-B, by prescribing detailed rules on incorporation, regulation, and winding up of cooperative societies, encroached on this exclusive domain!
Under the proviso to Art 368(2), any constitutional amendment affecting matters relating to the distribution of legislative powers between Union and States requires ratification by at least half the state legislatures — this ratification was NOT obtained for the 97th Amendment!
The Gujarat High Court held Part IX-B unconstitutional on this ground, and the Supreme Court, in Union of India vs Rajendra N. Shah (2021), largely upheld this reasoning!
The Supreme Court held that the amendments to Art 19(1)(c) and the addition of Art 43B (a DPSP, inherently non-binding and of general application) were VALID, as they did not require state ratification!
However, Part IX-B was struck down as unconstitutional insofar as it applied to STATE cooperative societies, for want of the necessary ratification!
As a result, Part IX-B's detailed provisions now survive only in relation to MULTI-STATE cooperative societies, which fall under the Union List (a subject squarely within Parliament's exclusive competence), and not state-specific cooperative societies!
Summary
The 97th Amendment (2011) added Art 19(1)(c) (fundamental right to form cooperative societies), Part IX-B (Art 243ZH-243ZT, detailed cooperative governance rules), and Art 43B (DPSP promoting cooperative autonomy) — driven by the need to curb government interference, mismanagement, and delayed elections in cooperatives, mirroring the 73rd/74th Amendments' protections for local bodies. Since cooperative societies fall under the State List (Entry 32), Part IX-B's application to state cooperatives required ratification by half the states under Art 368(2) proviso, which was never obtained. In Union of India vs Rajendra N. Shah (2021), the Supreme Court upheld the Gujarat High Court's view that Part IX-B is unconstitutional as applied to state cooperative societies, while confirming that Art 19(1)(c) and Art 43B remain valid. Part IX-B's provisions now apply only to multi-state cooperative societies (a Union List subject).
