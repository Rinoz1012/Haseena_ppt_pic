Main Notes
The Ghaznavids
Mahmud of Ghazni led repeated raids into India between roughly 1000-1027 CE, primarily motivated by plunder rather than territorial annexation!
His raid on the Somnath temple is particularly notorious for the wealth seized and the destruction caused!
The Rajput States
During this period, North India (particularly Rajasthan) was fragmented into numerous regional Rajput kingdoms. These states frequently fought among themselves as much as against external threats — a political disunity that would prove critical when facing the later, more determined Turkish invasions!
The Turkish Conquest of North India
Unlike Mahmud of Ghazni's plunder-focused raids, Muhammad Ghori's campaigns, beginning later in the 12th century, were aimed at establishing lasting territorial control over North India!
The Battle of Tarain
Two decisive battles were fought at Tarain. In the First Battle of Tarain (1191), Prithviraj Chauhan defeated Muhammad Ghori!
However, in the Second Battle of Tarain (1192), Ghori returned with a better-prepared force and decisively defeated Prithviraj Chauhan — this second battle proved pivotal in opening the path for Turkish conquest of North India!
Turkish Conquest of the Ganga Valley
Following the victory at Tarain, Qutb-ud-din Aibak, Ghori's trusted general, extended Turkish control further into the Gangetic plains, laying the groundwork for what would soon become the Delhi Sultanate!
Causes of the Defeat of the Rajputs
Several factors contributed to the Rajput defeat: persistent political disunity among Rajput states that prevented a coordinated response, military tactics and technology (particularly cavalry organization) that had fallen behind the more mobile and disciplined Turkish forces, and the general absence of any unified North Indian response to the invading forces!
Summary
The period 1000-1200 CE was marked by escalating conflict between external invaders and North Indian powers. Mahmud of Ghazni's early raids (c. 1000-1027) were plunder-driven, notably targeting the Somnath temple, while the fragmented Rajput states of this era often fought each other rather than presenting a unified front. Muhammad Ghori's later campaigns aimed at actual conquest, culminating in the pivotal Battles of Tarain — the Rajputs won the First (1191) but lost decisively in the Second (1192), opening the way for Qutb-ud-din Aibak's subsequent conquest of the Gangetic valley. The Rajput defeat is attributed to political disunity, outdated military tactics, and lack of coordinated resistance — setting the stage for the establishment of the Delhi Sultanate.
