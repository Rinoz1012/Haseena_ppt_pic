Main Notes
Establishment
NHRC was established under the Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993 (PHRA) — a statutory, not constitutional, body!
Composition
Chairperson — after the 2019 Amendment, may be a serving or retired Chief Justice of India, or a serving or retired Supreme Court judge (earlier, only a retired CJI could be Chairperson)!
Members — increased from 2 to 3 full-time members by the 2019 Amendment, at least one of whom must be a woman!
Ex-officio members include the Chairpersons of NCSC, NCST, NCW, NCM, NCPCR, and the Commission for Persons with Disabilities (added via successive amendments)!
Appointed by the President on the recommendation of a committee comprising the PM, Speaker of Lok Sabha, Union Home Minister, Leaders of Opposition in both Houses, and Deputy Chairman of Rajya Sabha!
Functions
Inquires into complaints of human rights violations, either suo motu or on a petition by a victim/any person!
Can intervene in any court proceeding involving allegations of human rights violation, with court's approval!
Visits jails and other detention institutions to study living conditions of inmates!
Reviews constitutional and legal safeguards for human rights, and factors (including terrorism) that inhibit their enjoyment!
Studies treaties and international instruments on human rights, promotes research, and spreads human rights literacy!
Working
Complaints must generally be filed within one year of the alleged human rights violation!
After inquiry, NHRC may recommend compensation, prosecution, or other remedial action to the concerned government/authority!
Role
NHRC's role is purely recommendatory — its findings and recommendations are not legally binding on governments/authorities!
It may approach the Supreme Court or High Court for necessary directions/orders/writs to enforce its recommendations!
Problems
Often called a "toothless tiger" due to lack of enforcement power and inability to penalize violators!
Cannot directly investigate human rights violations by the armed forces — can only seek a report from the Central Government!
One-year time limit for complaints restricts its reach; also relies on government-deputed staff, limiting its independence and capacity!
Summary
NHRC (Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993) is a statutory body; composition expanded by the 2019 Amendment to allow serving/retired CJI or SC judges as Chairperson and increase members from 2 to 3 (one woman mandatory), plus ex-officio members from other rights commissions. Appointed by the President on a multi-party committee's recommendation; term reduced to 3 years/70 yrs (2019 Amendment), removable like a SC judge. Functions include inquiring into complaints, intervening in court cases, visiting detention facilities, and reviewing legal safeguards. Its role is purely recommendatory, and it faces criticism as a "toothless tiger" — no enforcement power, no direct probe into armed forces conduct, a strict 1-year complaint window, and dependence on government-deputed staff.
