Main Notes
Political Fragmentation
Following Mughal decline, India in the 18th century saw the rise of numerous successor states — Bengal, Awadh, and Hyderabad among the most prominent — alongside the expanding Maratha confederacy, emerging Sikh states in Punjab, and various Rajput states in Rajasthan!
Nature of Successor States
These successor states, while nominally continuing to acknowledge Mughal suzerainty, functioned in practice as independent regional powers with their own administrative and military systems!
Society and Economy
Indian society across these states continued to be organized along caste hierarchies, with considerable regional variation, and remained predominantly agrarian in economic structure!
Land revenue remained the primary source of state income across these various polities, while trade networks — both internal and external — continued to function despite the overall political fragmentation of the subcontinent!
Historiographical Debate
Historians have long debated how to characterize 18th century India — an older "Dark Age" view emphasized decline, disorder, and anarchy following Mughal collapse, while a more revisionist historical view emphasizes considerable regional vitality, continued economic activity, and the emergence of new centers of political and economic power during this period!
Setting the Stage for Colonialism
This period of political fragmentation and regional competition created conditions that European colonial powers, particularly the British, would increasingly exploit to expand their own political and economic influence in India!
Summary
18th century India was marked by significant political fragmentation following Mughal decline, with successor states (Bengal, Awadh, Hyderabad), the Maratha confederacy, Sikh states, and Rajput states all vying for regional power while nominally acknowledging Mughal suzerainty. Society remained caste-organized and agrarian, with land revenue as the primary economic base. Historians debate whether this period should be seen as a "Dark Age" of decline or, per revisionist scholarship, a period of regional vitality and economic continuity — regardless, this fragmentation created the political conditions that European colonial powers would exploit to expand their influence in India.
