Main Notes
The Vijayanagara Empire — Foundation and Conflict
Founded in the mid-14th century in the Deccan/South India, Vijayanagara arose partly as a response to the southward expansion of the Delhi Sultanate, and would remain a dominant South Indian power for over two centuries!
The Bahmani Kingdom — Expansion and Disintegration
The Bahmani Kingdom emerged as a breakaway Deccan Muslim sultanate and engaged in prolonged rivalry with Vijayanagara for regional dominance!
Mahmud Gawan, a notable Bahmani minister, implemented significant administrative reforms that strengthened the kingdom, though it eventually fragmented into several smaller successor states!
Climax of the Vijayanagara Empire and its Disintegration
Vijayanagara reached its peak under later rulers before eventually declining and disintegrating due to a combination of internal weaknesses and external military pressures!
The Advent of the Portuguese
The Portuguese arrived in India via a new sea route around the Cape of Good Hope, with Vasco da Gama's landmark voyage in 1498, subsequently establishing trading posts along India's western coast and initiating a new phase of direct European involvement in Indian trade and politics!
Summary
This period (1350-1565) was defined by the prolonged rivalry between the Vijayanagara Empire and the Bahmani Kingdom for control of the Deccan, with the Bahmanis eventually fragmenting into smaller successor states despite reforms under ministers like Mahmud Gawan. Vijayanagara itself reached a peak before eventual decline and disintegration. This era also marks a pivotal turning point in India's global contacts with the arrival of the Portuguese via a new sea route (Vasco da Gama, 1498), inaugurating a new phase of direct European engagement with India.
