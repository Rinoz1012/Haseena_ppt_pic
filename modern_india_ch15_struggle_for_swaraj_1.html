Main Notes
Post-World War I Context
The years following World War I saw growing nationalist unrest in India, fueled by unmet wartime promises of political reform, significant economic hardship, and repressive colonial measures such as the Rowlatt Act, which curtailed civil liberties!
The Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (1919)
This tragic and pivotal event saw British troops under General Dyer open fire on a peaceful gathering in Amritsar, causing massive civilian casualties and provoking nationwide outrage that significantly radicalized Indian political opinion!
Gandhi's Emergence as Leader
Mahatma Gandhi emerged during this period as the principal leader of the Indian nationalist movement, introducing distinctive methods of non-violent civil disobedience, which he termed Satyagraha, fundamentally reshaping nationalist strategy!
The Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-22)
This represented the first major nationwide mass movement organized under Gandhi's leadership, and was notably combined with the Khilafat Movement in an explicit effort to foster Hindu-Muslim political unity against British rule!
The Movement's Suspension
Following the Chauri Chaura incident, in which violence erupted against police, Gandhi made the significant decision to call off the Non-Cooperation Movement entirely, citing his firm commitment to strict non-violence as a guiding principle!
Legacy of this Period
This period firmly established mass civil disobedience as the central strategic approach of Indian nationalism going forward, significantly expanding political participation and mass mobilization well beyond the earlier, more elite-focused nationalist approaches of previous decades!
Summary
The struggle for Swaraj intensified dramatically after World War I amid unmet reform promises, economic hardship, and repressive measures, culminating in the tragic Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (1919) that radicalized Indian political opinion nationwide. Mahatma Gandhi emerged as the movement's principal leader, introducing non-violent Satyagraha methods through the landmark Non-Cooperation Movement (1920-22), which combined with the Khilafat Movement to promote Hindu-Muslim unity — though Gandhi called it off following the violent Chauri Chaura incident, reflecting his unwavering commitment to non-violence. This period's legacy was profound: it established mass civil disobedience as India's central nationalist strategy and dramatically expanded political participation beyond the earlier elite-focused approaches that had characterized the movement's initial decades.
