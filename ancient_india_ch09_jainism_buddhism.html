Main Notes
Causes for the Rise of New Religious Movements
Both Jainism and Buddhism emerged largely as reactions against the rigid varna system and the dominance of costly Brahmanical rituals (yajnas/sacrifices) that burdened ordinary people!
The rising trading class (Vaishyas) sought a new ideology that would grant them greater social recognition and status than the rigid Brahmanical hierarchy allowed!
Jainism
Mahavira, the 24th and last Tirthankara of Jainism, was born in Vaishali (Bihar), attained Kaivalya (omniscience), and passed away at Pava!
Core teachings center on the Triratna (Three Jewels) — Right Faith, Right Knowledge, and Right Conduct — along with five great vows (Mahavratas for monks, lesser Anuvratas for lay followers): Ahimsa (non-violence), Satya (truth), Asteya (non-stealing), Brahmacharya (celibacy), and Aparigraha (non-possession)!
Ahimsa (non-violence) is the most heavily emphasized principle in Jainism; the religion does not believe in a creator God, and places strong emphasis on asceticism and self-denial as the path to spiritual liberation!
Buddhism
Gautama Buddha was born in Lumbini (present-day Nepal), attained enlightenment at Bodh Gaya under the Bodhi tree, delivered his first sermon (the "Dharmachakra Pravartana") at Sarnath, and passed away (Mahaparinirvana) at Kushinagar!
His core teachings are encapsulated in the Four Noble Truths (suffering exists, has a cause, can be ended, and there is a path to end it) and the Eightfold Path (Right View, Intention, Speech, Action, Livelihood, Effort, Mindfulness, and Concentration), with the ultimate goal being Nirvana — liberation from the cycle of rebirth!
The Buddhist Sangha was a monastic order for monks and nuns, functioning through relatively democratic decision-making (consensus and voting), and importantly, was open to people of all varnas, unlike the rigid Brahmanical religious order!
Popularity and Later Decline of Buddhism
Buddhism gained widespread popularity due to its simple teachings (compared to complex Vedic rituals), its use of Pali/Prakrit (languages of the common people, rather than Sanskrit), its rejection of caste rigidity, and substantial royal patronage, notably from Emperor Ashoka!
Buddhism's decline in India over the centuries has been attributed to a revival of Brahmanical Hinduism (which absorbed many Buddhist followers, partly through the Bhakti movements), gradual loss of royal patronage, internal sectarian splits (Hinayana and Mahayana), and damage inflicted on monasteries by later foreign invasions!
Summary
Jainism and Buddhism arose as reactions against rigid varna hierarchy and costly Brahmanical rituals, appealing especially to the rising trading class. Mahavira (24th and last Tirthankara) taught the Triratna and five vows, with Ahimsa as the central principle and no belief in a creator God. Buddha's life is marked by four key locations — Lumbini (birth), Bodh Gaya (enlightenment), Sarnath (first sermon), Kushinagar (death) — and his teachings center on the Four Noble Truths and Eightfold Path, aiming at Nirvana. The Buddhist Sangha's openness to all varnas and use of Pali/Prakrit fueled its early popularity, while its later decline in India stemmed from Brahmanical revival, loss of patronage, sectarian splits, and invasions.
