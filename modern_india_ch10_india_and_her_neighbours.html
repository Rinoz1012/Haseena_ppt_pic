Main Notes
The "Forward Policy"
The British pursued a strategic "Forward Policy" aimed at expanding and securing India's frontiers against perceived external threats, motivated significantly by concerns over Russian expansion into Central Asia during this period!
Anglo-Afghan Relations
British relations with Afghanistan involved a series of Anglo-Afghan Wars, driven substantially by British anxiety over growing Russian influence in Afghanistan — a rivalry historians often term the "Great Game" between the British and Russian empires!
Burma
A series of Anglo-Burmese Wars were fought over this period, eventually culminating in the full British annexation of Burma into the broader British Indian administrative framework!
Nepal
The Anglo-Nepalese War (also called the Gurkha War) resulted in the Treaty of Sugauli, which formally defined the border relationship and terms of engagement between British India and Nepal!
Tibet
The British maintained significant strategic interest in Tibet, seeking to preserve it as a buffer zone and area of British influence as part of their broader frontier defense strategy!
Overall Theme
India's frontier policy during this period was substantially shaped by broader great-power rivalry, particularly between Britain and Russia, rather than purely by regional Indian concerns or interests!
Summary
British policy toward India's neighbours during this period was substantially shaped by the "Forward Policy" — an approach driven significantly by concerns over Russian expansion into Central Asia, famously termed the "Great Game." This manifested in a series of Anglo-Afghan Wars, Anglo-Burmese Wars (leading to Burma's eventual annexation), the Anglo-Nepalese War (resulting in the Treaty of Sugauli), and sustained British interest in maintaining influence over Tibet as a strategic buffer. Overall, India's frontier policy during this period reflected broader geopolitical great-power rivalry between Britain and Russia rather than purely regional considerations.
