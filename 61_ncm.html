Main Notes
Establishment
National Commission for Minorities (NCM) was established under the National Commission for Minorities Act, 1992 — a statutory body!
The Central Government has notified six communities as minorities: Muslims, Christians, Sikhs, Buddhists, Zoroastrians (Parsis), and Jains (Jains added in 2014)!
Composition
Consists of a Chairperson, a Vice-Chairperson, and 5 members, all nominated by the Central Government!
Members are chosen from persons of eminence, ability, and integrity, and must belong to minority communities — but not all from the same community, ensuring balanced representation!
Function
Evaluates the progress of development of minorities under the Union and States!
Monitors the working of safeguards provided for minorities in the Constitution, laws, and government policy!
Makes recommendations for effective implementation of these safeguards, and looks into specific complaints regarding deprivation of rights and safeguards of minorities!
Causes studies to be undertaken on issues relating to discrimination against minorities, and suggests appropriate measures for their socio-economic development!
Suggests appropriate steps to be taken by the Central or State Governments in respect of any minority community!
Powers
While investigating any complaint, NCM enjoys all the powers of a civil court — similar to NCW, NCPCR, NHRC — including summoning witnesses and requiring production of documents!
Report
NCM submits periodic (usually annual) reports and any other reports it deems necessary to the Central Government!
The Central Government causes these reports to be laid before Parliament, along with a memorandum explaining the action taken and, where recommendations are not accepted, the reasons for non-acceptance!
Summary
NCM (National Commission for Minorities Act, 1992) is a statutory body overseeing 6 notified minority communities (Muslims, Christians, Sikhs, Buddhists, Parsis, and Jains — added 2014). Composed of a Chairperson + Vice-Chairperson + 5 members, all nominated by the Central Government and required to belong to minority communities themselves (not all from one community). Functions include evaluating minority development progress, monitoring constitutional/legal safeguards, examining complaints, and recommending socio-economic measures, backed by civil-court investigative powers. Reports are submitted to the Central Government and laid before Parliament with an action-taken memorandum.
