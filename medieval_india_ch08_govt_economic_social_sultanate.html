Main Notes
The Sultan and Central Administration
The Sultan was the head of state, theoretically owing nominal allegiance to the Caliph, though in practice exercised near-complete independent authority!
The Wazir headed central administration, particularly overseeing finance through the Diwan-i-Wizarat, supported by various other departments handling military, judicial, and religious affairs!
Local Administration
The empire was divided into provinces called Iqtas, governed by Muqtis or Walis, who collected revenue and maintained troops in exchange for administrative authority over their assigned territory; these were further subdivided into smaller administrative units!
Economic and Social Life
The economy was predominantly agrarian, with land revenue forming the main source of state income. Trade and merchant classes remained active, particularly in urban centers!
Peasants and Rural Gentry; Town Life
Peasants bore a heavy tax burden under the Sultanate's revenue demands, while local rural elites — zamindars and chieftains — retained some degree of local influence and often served as intermediaries in revenue collection!
Urban centers hosted diverse populations including slaves, artisans, and merchants, with caste-like social hierarchies persisting even within this Islamic political framework!
Nature of the State and Religious Freedom
Historians debate whether the Sultanate should be characterized as a theocratic state or a more pragmatic, relatively secular administration in practice!
Non-Muslims, mainly Hindus, were generally permitted to practice their religion, though subject to the jizya tax in certain periods!
Summary
The Delhi Sultanate's administration combined central authority (Sultan, Wazir, Diwan-i-Wizarat) with a decentralized provincial system (Iqtas governed by Muqtis/Walis). Economically, it relied heavily on agrarian land revenue, with peasants bearing a heavy tax burden while rural elites retained local influence. Urban life featured a mix of slaves, artisans, and merchants within persisting social hierarchies. The nature of the Sultanate as a state remains debated among historians — between theocratic and pragmatic characterizations — though religious freedom for non-Muslims was generally maintained, subject to periodic taxation like the jizya.
