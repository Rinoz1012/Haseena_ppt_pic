Main Notes
The Mamluk Sultans
The first dynasty of the Delhi Sultanate is often called the "Slave Dynasty" (Mamluk), as several early rulers had originally been enslaved before rising to power — this was a recognized pathway to power in the Turkish/Central Asian political tradition of the time!
Struggle for a Strong Monarchy
Early Delhi Sultans faced persistent challenges to their authority from powerful nobles, particularly a group of forty influential Turkish nobles known as the Chahalgani, who often resisted centralized royal control!
Iltutmish (1210-1236)
Iltutmish is widely regarded as the real founder of the Delhi Sultanate as a stable, consolidated state. He introduced the Iqta system (assignment of land revenue to nobles/officials in lieu of salary, in exchange for military/administrative service) and issued standardized currency — the silver tanka and copper jital — helping establish a more organized administrative and economic framework!
Raziya
Iltutmish's daughter Raziya became one of the rare female rulers of the Sultanate, but her reign was short-lived due to significant opposition from the nobility, who resisted acknowledging a woman's authority!
Balban (1246-1287)
Balban worked to restore and strengthen royal authority, decisively crushing the power of the Chahalgani nobility!
He promoted a strong "Theory of Kingship" emphasizing the divine right and exalted status of the Sultan, and is remembered as a harsh but highly effective administrator who stabilized the Sultanate!
The Mongols and the Northwest Frontier
Throughout this period, the emerging Mongol threat along the northwest frontier required constant defensive vigilance from the Delhi Sultans, shaping military and administrative priorities!
Summary
The first century of the Delhi Sultanate (1200-1300) was defined by the Mamluk ("Slave") dynasty's struggle to establish a strong, stable monarchy against persistent noble resistance. Iltutmish (1210-36) is considered the true founder of a consolidated Sultanate, introducing the Iqta system and standardized coinage; his daughter Raziya's short reign highlighted noble resistance to female rule; and Balban (1246-87) ultimately crushed noble power (the Chahalgani) and asserted a strong theory of divine kingship. Throughout this period, the Sultanate also had to manage the emerging Mongol threat on the northwest frontier while dealing with repeated internal rebellions during its efforts at territorial consolidation.
