Main Notes
Two Types of Mahajanapadas
The sixteen Mahajanapadas of this period were of two broad political types: monarchies (such as Magadha, Kosala, Avanti, and Vatsa), governed by hereditary kings, and ganasanghas or republics (such as the Vajji confederacy and the Mallas), governed through councils of clan elders!
Features of the Ganasanghas
Ganasanghas were ruled by assemblies composed of clan heads and elders rather than a single hereditary monarch — the Vajji confederacy, with Vaishali as its capital and the Lichchhavi clan prominent within it, is a well-known example!
While relatively more egalitarian than monarchical states, these were not equivalent to modern democracies — political participation was generally limited to clan elites rather than extending to the wider population!
State Administration
Kings in the larger monarchical states were assisted by ministers and revenue officials, with an increasingly developed bureaucracy managing state affairs!
Taxation included land revenue (called Bhaga, typically around one-sixth of agricultural produce) as well as taxes on trade!
Varna Society
The four-fold varna system, which had crystallized during the Later Vedic period, remained rigid during this age, dividing society into Brahmanas, Kshatriyas, Vaishyas, and Shudras!
Shudras, positioned as laborers and servants, faced increasingly restricted rights, and this period also saw the emergence of the concept of "untouchability," referring to certain groups placed entirely outside the varna framework!
Slavery (Dasa-Dasi System)
Slavery existed in ancient Indian society, but differed considerably from the chattel slavery systems of ancient Greece and Rome — it was often more akin to domestic servitude, and enslaved individuals could sometimes gain freedom!
Trade Guilds (Shrenis)
Merchants and craftsmen organized themselves into guilds called Shrenis, which regulated trade practices, product quality, and pricing!
These guilds grew increasingly important as urbanization and trade expanded significantly during this period!
Summary
The age of the Buddha saw Mahajanapadas organized as either monarchies (Magadha, Kosala, Avanti, Vatsa) or ganasanghas/republics (like the Vajji confederacy centered at Vaishali) — the latter more egalitarian but still elite-dominated, not fully democratic. State administration featured growing bureaucracies and land-revenue-based taxation (Bhaga). Varna society remained rigidly four-fold, with Shudras facing increasing restrictions and the concept of untouchability emerging for groups outside the varna system. Slavery (the Dasa-Dasi system) existed but differed from Greco-Roman chattel slavery, often resembling domestic servitude with paths to freedom. Trade guilds (Shrenis) grew increasingly significant as urbanization and commerce expanded during this age.
