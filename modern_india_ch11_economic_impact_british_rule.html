Main Notes
Deindustrialization
British rule led to significant deindustrialization in India, as traditional handicraft industries — particularly the textile industry — suffered severe decline in the face of competition from cheaper, machine-made British manufactured goods!
The "Drain of Wealth" Theory
Indian nationalist economists, notably Dadabhai Naoroji and R.C. Dutt, developed the influential "Drain of Wealth" theory, arguing that British colonial rule systematically extracted wealth from India — through mechanisms like home charges, unequal trade terms, and remittances to Britain — without providing adequate economic return to India!
Commercialization of Agriculture
This period saw a significant shift toward commercialized agriculture, with increasing cultivation of cash crops such as cotton, indigo, and jute for export purposes, often coming at the expense of food crop production and contributing to food security vulnerabilities!
Railways
While railways were built primarily to serve British commercial interests (facilitating the export of raw materials) and strategic/military interests (facilitating troop movement), they also had certain incidental developmental side-effects on Indian connectivity and, to some extent, economic integration!
Famines
India experienced a number of recurring, often devastating famines during the period of British rule, which nationalist critics frequently linked to exploitative economic policies, excessive revenue demands, and the broader structural economic drain caused by colonial rule!
Historiographical Debate
There remains significant historiographical debate over the overall economic impact of British rule — some historians point to certain developmental achievements (such as railway infrastructure), while others, particularly nationalist and dependency-theory perspectives, view British economic policy as overwhelmingly exploitative and detrimental to India's long-term economic development!
Summary
British rule had profound and largely negative economic impacts on India: deindustrialization devastated traditional handicraft industries, especially textiles, under competition from British manufactured goods, while nationalist economists like Dadabhai Naoroji and R.C. Dutt articulated the influential "Drain of Wealth" theory to describe systematic economic exploitation. The commercialization of agriculture toward export cash crops often undermined food security, contributing to recurring famines that nationalist critics linked directly to exploitative colonial policies. While railways served primarily British commercial and strategic interests, they had some incidental developmental benefits — though overall, significant historiographical debate persists over whether British rule's economic impact should be assessed as net beneficial or overwhelmingly exploitative.
