Main Notes
The Battle of Plassey (1757)
This decisive battle saw the English East India Company, led by Robert Clive, defeat Siraj-ud-Daulah, the Nawab of Bengal — a victory secured partly through political intrigue and the defection of key Bengali commanders, and widely regarded as the foundational moment of British political power in India!
The Battle of Buxar (1764)
This battle further confirmed British military dominance, as Company forces defeated a combined force representing Bengal, Awadh, and the Mughal emperor himself!
The Treaty of Allahabad (1765)
Following Buxar, this treaty granted the East India Company the Diwani rights — the right to collect land revenue — over Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa, marking a crucial transition from a trading company to a territorial revenue-collecting power!
The Subsidiary Alliance System
Under this system, the Company effectively subordinated allied Indian states by stationing British troops within their territories (paid for by the allied ruler), significantly curtailing these states' independent military and political capacity!
The Doctrine of Lapse
This policy allowed the Company to annex a princely state if its ruler died without a natural (biological) heir, disregarding the traditional Indian practice of adoption to continue a royal lineage — a policy that generated considerable resentment among Indian rulers!
Progressive Annexation
Through a combination of direct military conquest, the Subsidiary Alliance system, and the Doctrine of Lapse, the East India Company progressively expanded its control across most of the Indian subcontinent by the mid-19th century!
Summary
The British conquest of India began decisively with the Battle of Plassey (1757, Robert Clive's victory over Siraj-ud-Daulah) and was confirmed by the Battle of Buxar (1764), leading to the Treaty of Allahabad (1765) granting the Company Diwani rights over Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa — transforming it from a trading company into a territorial power. Subsequent expansion relied heavily on the Subsidiary Alliance system (subordinating allied states through stationed troops) and the Doctrine of Lapse (annexing states lacking natural heirs), through which the Company progressively extended control across most of India by the mid-19th century.
