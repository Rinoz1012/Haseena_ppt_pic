Main Notes
Property of the Union and the States
Art 294 provides that, upon the Constitution's commencement, all property, assets, rights, and liabilities of the former Dominion of India and the Provinces passed to the Union and the corresponding States respectively!
Art 295 makes similar succession provisions for other cases, such as property/rights/liabilities of former Indian princely states that acceded to India!
Art 296 provides that any property accruing by way of escheat or lapse (i.e., where there is no rightful heir), or as bona vacantia (ownerless property), vests in the Union if situated in a Union Territory, or in the State if situated within its territory — subject to any specific law made on the matter!
Art 297 provides that all lands, minerals, and other things of value within the territorial waters of India, and the resources of the continental shelf and Exclusive Economic Zone, vest in the Union and are held for the purposes of the Union!
Suits by or against the Government
Art 300 allows the Union of India or a State Government to sue, or be sued, in the name of "Union of India" or the respective state's name, in relation to their affairs — extending a principle similar to what existed for the Dominion of India and the Provinces before the Constitution!
This extends the concept of governmental (sovereign) liability, though historically courts recognized an exception for acts done in the exercise of "sovereign functions" (as in the Kasturilal case, where the state was held not liable for loss caused by negligence of police officials acting in a sovereign capacity)!
However, subsequent Supreme Court jurisprudence has significantly narrowed this sovereign-function exception, increasingly favouring the award of compensation where fundamental/legal rights have been violated by state action, regardless of whether the act was characterized as "sovereign"!
Suits Against Public Officials
Art 299 provides that contracts made in the exercise of the executive power of the Union or a State are expressed to be made by the President or the Governor, and executed by persons and in the manner as the President/Governor may direct or authorize!
Neither the President nor the Governor is personally liable for such contracts — they act purely in their constitutional/official capacity!
Public officials may, however, be sued in their personal capacity for acts done outside the scope of their official duty, or done in bad faith!
Acts done in good faith in the discharge of official duty generally enjoy statutory protection — for instance, prosecution of public servants for acts done in the discharge of official duty typically requires prior sanction under provisions like Section 197 of the CrPC (now similarly under the BNSS)!
Summary
Art 294-297 govern succession and vesting of government property: Art 294 (succession from Dominion/Provinces to Union/States), Art 295 (succession from other entities like former princely states), Art 296 (escheat/bona vacantia property vesting in Union or State), and Art 297 (territorial waters/continental shelf/EEZ resources vesting in the Union). Art 300 allows the Union/States to sue and be sued, extending sovereign liability — though the historical "sovereign function" exception (Kasturilal case) has been progressively narrowed by later Supreme Court rulings favoring compensation for rights violations. Under Art 299, government contracts are made in the name of the President/Governor, who bear no personal liability; public officials can be personally sued for bad-faith or out-of-duty acts, while good-faith official acts enjoy statutory protection (e.g., sanction requirements before prosecution).
