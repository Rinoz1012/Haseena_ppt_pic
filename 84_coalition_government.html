Main Notes
Meaning
A coalition government is one formed by an alliance of two or more political parties, none of which individually commands a majority in the legislature!
Coalition governments are especially common in multi-party systems like India, where no single party frequently secures an outright majority!
Features
Power and ministerial portfolios are shared among the allied parties, based on negotiated arrangements!
Coalitions typically operate on the basis of a Common Minimum Programme (CMP) — a negotiated policy agenda that all coalition partners agree to support, papering over ideological differences on other issues!
Coalition governance requires continuous negotiation and compromise among partner parties on both policy and political matters!
Formation
Coalitions may be formed as pre-poll alliances, where parties agree to contest elections together and share seats before the results are known, or as post-poll alliances, where parties combine after election results show no single party has a majority!
Typically, the leader of the largest party in the coalition, or a pre-agreed leader, becomes the Prime Minister or Chief Minister!
Merits
Coalition governments better reflect the diversity of political opinion in a multi-party society!
They prevent excessive dominance of a single party or ideology, and encourage a culture of consensus-building in policymaking!
They give a political voice and share of power to regional and smaller parties that might otherwise be marginalized under single-party rule!
Demerits
Coalition governments can suffer from political instability, with frequent changes of government if alliances break down!
Constant negotiation among partners can lead to policy paralysis or excessive compromise on important reforms!
Smaller coalition partners, despite holding relatively few seats, can wield disproportionate bargaining power, since their withdrawal of support can bring down the government!
There is often tension between maintaining "coalition dharma" (loyalty to alliance commitments) and preserving ideological consistency for individual parties!
Summary
A coalition government is formed by an alliance of parties, none of which has a majority alone, common in multi-party systems. Coalitions share power/portfolios, operate via a negotiated Common Minimum Programme, and require ongoing compromise. Formation can be pre-poll or post-poll, with the largest/pre-agreed party's leader typically becoming PM/CM. Merits include reflecting political diversity, preventing single-party dominance, and empowering regional/smaller parties, while demerits include political instability, policy paralysis, disproportionate bargaining power of small partners, and tension between coalition loyalty and ideological consistency.
