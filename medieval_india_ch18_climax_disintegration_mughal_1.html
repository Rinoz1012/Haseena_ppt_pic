Main Notes
Problems of Succession
The Mughal Empire faced recurring and often violent succession conflicts among royal claimants, which repeatedly disrupted political stability and, over time, weakened the overall strength of central authority!
Aurangzeb's Reign — His Religious Policy
Aurangzeb pursued notably more orthodox and less religiously tolerant policies compared to his predecessors, including the reintroduction of the jizya tax on non-Muslims, marking a significant departure from earlier Mughal approaches to religious pluralism!
Political Developments — North India; Northeast and East India
Aurangzeb's reign witnessed various significant regional political developments and challenges across North India as well as the northeastern and eastern regions of the empire!
Popular Revolts and Movements for Regional Independence — Jats, Afghans and Sikhs
Various regional groups, including the Jats, Afghans, and Sikhs, increasingly asserted their autonomy and resistance against Mughal authority during this period, reflecting growing centrifugal pressures within the empire!
Relations with the Rajputs — Breach with Marwar and Mewar
Aurangzeb's relationship with key Rajput states, particularly Marwar and Mewar, deteriorated significantly during his reign, breaking from the generally more cooperative relationship that had characterized earlier Mughal-Rajput relations!
Summary
The early phase of Mughal decline under Aurangzeb was marked by recurring succession conflicts, a shift toward more orthodox and less tolerant religious policy (including reintroduction of the jizya), and growing regional resistance movements from groups like the Jats, Afghans, and Sikhs. Aurangzeb's relations with key Rajput states, particularly Marwar and Mewar, also deteriorated significantly, breaking from earlier patterns of Mughal-Rajput cooperation and contributing to the mounting internal pressures that would increasingly challenge Mughal authority.
